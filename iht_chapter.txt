A Basic principles of inheritance tax
For many years, the capital tax payable on death was ‘estate duty’. In 1974, estate duty was
replaced by ‘capital transfer tax’, which applied to gifts during life as well as on death.
• The tax underwent many changes and eventually, the legislation was codified in the
Capital Transfer Tax Act (CTT Act) 1984.
• In 1986, Parliament made further substantial amendments to the tax, greatly reducing its
application to lifetime gifts, and renamed it inheritance tax (IHT). The CTT Act became
the Inheritance Tax Act (IHTA) 1984.
IHT is charged on certain transfers of property or ‘value’.
• A person who makes a transfer is described as the ‘transferor’ or ‘donor’. The two terms
are generally interchangeable in an IHT context.
• The person to whom property is transferred is described as the ‘transferee’ or ‘donee’.
Again, the two terms have the same meaning in practice.
A1 Inheritance tax rates
IHT is payable on transfers in excess of a nil rate band, currently £325,000 (2026/27). The
nil rate band will remain unchanged at £325,000 until 5 April 2031.

of the nil rate band at the date of the second death.

A2 Residence nil rate band
The residence nil rate band (RNRB) is in addition to the £325,000 nil rate band. It is
intended to protect, at least partially, the family home from IHT, and is £175,000 in 2026/27.
The RNRB will remain unchanged at £175,000 until 5 April 2031.
The RNRB can be set only against the chargeable estates of those who died on or after
6 April 2017.
• The RNRB is available only where a home is inherited on death by direct descendants,
who may be the child (including a stepchild, adopted child or foster child) of the deceased
or their lineal descendants. It can also include the spouse or civil partner of one of these
lineal descendants.
• Any unused RNRB on first death can be transferred to a surviving spouse or civil partner
– even if the first spouse or civil partner died before 6 April 2017.
• The RNRB can be transferred regardless of whether the estate of the first spouse or civil
partner to die included a residence.
• The value of the home is calculated after deducting any repayment mortgage or interest-
only mortgage secured on that property.
• If a home is valued at less than the available RNRB, the RNRB is reduced to the value of
the home.
• The RNRB is not available against lifetime transfers becoming chargeable as a result of
the donor’s death within seven years.
• The availability of the RNRB is protected where a person has downsized or ceased to
own their home after 7 July 2015 – provided assets of an equivalent value are then
passed to direct descendants on death.
• There is a tapered withdrawal of the RNRB for estates with a net value of more than £2m
(after deducting any liabilities but before reliefs and exemptions). The withdrawal is at the
rate of £1 for every £2 over the taper threshold. For example, for an estate with a net
value of £2.1m, the RNRB for 2026/27 would be restricted to £125,000 (i.e. £175,000 –
((£2.1m – £2m) ÷ 2)). Any RNRB transferred from a predeceased spouse or civil partner
is included within this tapered withdrawal.
• The tapered withdrawal also applies to the first death RNRB transferred from a spouse
or civil partner if their estate had a net value exceeding £2m, even if death was before
6 April 2017.
• Relief is available only in respect of one home, but it is possible to nominate which home
should qualify where there is more than one in an estate. However, a property that was
never a residence of the deceased, such as a buy-to-let property, does not qualify.

Example 4.1
Lucy
Lucy died on 22 July 2026, leaving to her children an estate valued at £950,000. The
estate included a residence valued at £450,000 on which there was an outstanding
interest-only mortgage of £160,000, and Lucy had made no lifetime gifts.
Lucy’s husband had died on 12 May 2010, with all of his nil rate band being used. His
estate was valued at less than £2m.
• The available RNRB to Lucy’s estate is £350,000 (i.e. £175,000 + £175,000).
• However, only £290,000 (i.e. £450,000 – £160,000) can be used because this is the
value of the residence (after deducting the mortgage).
• The IHT liability on Lucy’s estate will therefore be:
(£950,000 – £290,000 – £325,000) × 40% = £134,000.

A3 The cumulation principle
IHT is a cumulative tax applying to transfers during life and on death. The cumulation
principle works as follows:
• All chargeable transfers over a seven-year period are added together and tax is payable
once the nil rate band is exceeded.
• A transfer drops out of the cumulation once it is more than seven years old.
• However, a transfer that has dropped out may still be relevant when establishing the
amount of IHT payable on a transfer made within seven years of death. This may
be where:
– A potentially exempt transfer (PET) made within seven years of the original transfer
becomes chargeable on the transferor’s death.
– There is additional IHT to pay on a chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT) made within
seven years of the transferor’s death.
• Only CLTs have to be taken into account in this way, as PETs made more than seven
years before the date of death are completely exempt.
• When death occurs, the value of the estate left is added to the total of CLTs and PETs in
the previous seven years, in order to calculate the tax due.
Example 4.2
IHT on an estate
The IHT on an estate of £525,000 (including a residence inherited by children) is currently
£10,000 (i.e. (£525,000 – £175,000 – £325,000) × 40%) if there were no chargeable
transfers in the previous seven years.
However, if there had been £100,000 of chargeable transfers in the seven years before
the death, only £225,000 of the nil rate band would be left, and so the IHT on the estate
would be £50,000 (i.e. (£525,000 – £175,000 – £225,000) × 40%).
If the previous seven years’ chargeable transfers totalled £325,000, the tax on the estate
would be £140,000 (i.e. (£525,000 – £175,000) × 40%), because the nil rate band would
have been fully used. The RNRB is not available against lifetime transfers.

A4 Liability to inheritance tax
Individuals who are long-term resident in the UK are liable to IHT on all their property,
whether it is situated in the UK or elsewhere.
• For IHT purposes, long-term residence means being resident in the UK for at least 10 out
of the previous 20 tax years. For example, an individual will be long-term resident in the
UK from 6 April 2026 if they have been UK tax resident in at least 10 tax years during the
20-year period from 2006/07 to 2025/26 inclusive.
• There are different rules for those aged 20 or younger; they are long-term resident if they
have been resident in the UK for at least 50% of the tax years since their birth.
• If a person is not long-term resident in the UK, IHT only applies to property that is situated
in the UK.
• The long-term residency rules apply from 6 April 2025. Compared with the previous basis,
they provide certainty. For example, an individual who leaves the UK, having lived there
all their life, knows they will cease to be subject to UK IHT on their overseas assets once
they have been non-UK resident for ten tax years.
Refer to
See Residence in the UK on page 5/2 and Liability to tax of qualifying new residents on
page 5/7 for more on residence and qualifying new residents.
When an individual who is neither resident nor long-term resident in the UK dies, any foreign
currency bank account held with the Bank of England or any other recognised UK bank is left
out of the estate for IHT purposes.
Gilts acquired after 5 April 2013 are generally treated as excluded property if they are owned
by an individual who is non-UK resident, regardless of their long-term residence status.
A5 Transactions to which inheritance tax applies
IHT applies generally to lifetime transfers that reduce the value of the transferor’s estate and
on the whole estate at death. Estate means the total sum of a person’s assets less liabilities,
whether on death or during lifetime.
A transfer of value is a reduction in the donor’s estate as a result of a transfer. Specifically,
it is defined as any disposition made by a transferor by which the value of the estate
immediately afterwards is less than it would have been if the disposition had not been made.
When the transferor pays the tax on a gift, the transfer of value includes the tax.
A5A Loss to the estate
IHT is charged on the value transferred by a ‘chargeable transfer’, which means any transfer
of value made by an individual who is not exempt.
Refer to
See Exempt transfers on page 4/7 for exempt transfers.
• An interest-free loan is not a transfer of value if it is repayable on demand or on death.
• Any transfer that is allowable in computing a person’s profits for income tax is exempt.
A contribution for an employee to an approved occupational pension scheme or personal
pension is likewise exempt.
• Usually, when someone makes a gift, there is no difference between the increase in the
value of the recipient’s estate and the reduction in the value of the estate of the person
who made the gift. In some cases, however, there can be a significant difference.

Example 4.3
Joe
Joe gives away one chair worth £1,000 in a set of twelve antique chairs worth £20,000 as
a set. Breaking up the set would reduce the value of the remaining chairs to, say, £15,000.
For the purposes of IHT, the transfer of value caused by the gift of the chair is the £5,000
reduction in the value of the set of chairs, not the £1,000 value of the chair to the recipient.
Mary makes a gift of 5% of a property company to Joe, who owns no other shares in
the company. This gift takes her from having a majority holding of 52% to a minority 47%
holding. As a majority holding, her shares were worth £520,000; but now the holding is
worth perhaps only £350,000, even though the 5% holding itself is worth very little, say,
£10,000 or less, to Joe. The gift has reduced her estate by £170,000 (i.e. £520,000 –
£350,000) because she has lost control of the company.
A5B Gratuitous intent
IHT is charged on dispositions that are gifts or part gifts. There can be no IHT on
commercial transactions if full consideration is received because there is then no loss to
the estate.
• There is a specific exemption for transactions if:
– There was no intent to confer any gratuitous benefit.
– The transaction was either at arm’s length between unconnected persons or was such
as might be expected to be made in those circumstances.
• An individual would therefore not pay IHT if they made a ‘bad bargain’.
• Commercial transactions between connected persons, e.g. members of the same family
or partners in the same business, are generally looked at carefully to check that they are
on an arm’s length basis.
A5C Failure to exercise a right
IHT is chargeable when a deliberate omission to exercise a right reduces the value of the
estate. This includes failure to collect a debt. The date of the transfer is the latest time on
which the right could have been exercised.
A5D Associated operations
There are provisions to combat avoidance devices using a series of transactions. These
‘associated operations’ provisions cover transactions whereby a transfer of value is made by
two or more operations of any kind affecting the same property. The rules attempt to tax the
transaction as a whole, disregarding the intermediate steps.

The basics of IHT are covered on the GOV.UK website: www.gov.uk/inheritance-tax.
B Lifetime transfers and transfers on death
A lifetime transfer could be:
• exempt;
• a PET;
• chargeable but not taxable; or
• chargeable and taxable.
A lifetime transfer that is neither exempt nor potentially exempt is chargeable. If this takes
the seven-year cumulation total over the nil rate band, the excess is taxable at the lifetime
rate of 20%.

B1 Exempt transfers
The impact of IHT is reduced by a number of exemptions and reliefs. It is important to
understand the distinction between exemptions and reliefs.
• An exemption means that the transfer does not count as a chargeable transfer and is
therefore neither taxed nor included in the cumulation for the future.
• A relief reduces the value of a chargeable transfer. It does not remove the transfer from
the tax regime but merely reduces its value.
This section looks at the main exempt transfers. These exemptions are not mutually
exclusive except for small gifts. A transfer may fall under more than one exemption and
so be freed from tax partly by one exemption and partly by another.
Remember that although all the reliefs and exemptions are in addition to the nil rate band
(£325,000 in 2026/27), they are quite different.
The nil rate band is a 0% rate of tax, whilst (for example) the £325,000 cap on transfers to a
spouse who is not long-term resident is an exemption.
B1A Transfers between spouses and civil partners
Transfers between spouses and civil partners, during life and on death, are exempt.
The spouse has to be legally married and not a ‘common law’ husband or wife. Civil partners
must be registered under the Civil Partnership Act 2004.
Refer to
See Spouses and civil partners on page 3/3 for transfers between partners.
• Transfers are exempt even if the donor and spouse or civil partner are not living together.
However, CGT has different rules, which can create difficulties when a couple are
separated, whether or not divorce or dissolution of civil partnership is contemplated.
• There is usually no IHT on transfers when a marriage or civil partnership breaks down,
as long as they take place before the decree absolute. Even after this event, it may be
possible to argue that there is no gratuitous intent.
• If the transferor is long-term resident in the UK but the spouse or civil partner is not, the
exemption is limited to a lifetime total value of £325,000 – the same amount as the nil
rate band. It is possible for a spouse or civil partner who is not long-term resident to elect
to be treated as long-term resident for IHT purposes. Making an election avoids the cap
on exempt transfers to a spouse or civil partner who is not long-term resident, but it also
means that the spouse or civil partner is taxed on their worldwide property rather than just
property situated in the UK.
Example 4.4
Angela and Martina
Angela gives her civil partner Martina £600,000. Martina is not long-term resident in the
UK. Assuming no other transfers, there would be no IHT payable in respect of that gift
because:
• £325,000 would be covered by the exemption for a non-long-term resident spouse or
civil partner;
• £6,000 would be covered by two annual exemptions; and
• the remaining £269,000 would be a PET (falling within the nil rate band of £325,000).
If Angela died within seven years, the amount of estate that is taxable would be increased
by £269,000, as £269,000 (i.e. £600,000 less the £325,000 and £6,000 exemptions) of the
nil rate band would not be available.

B1B Annual exemption
The annual exemption is £3,000. This means that a transferor may make lifetime transfers
exempt from IHT up to a total value of £3,000 in any one tax year.
If the whole £3,000 is not used in any tax year, the balance can be carried forward to the
next year. For example, if someone transfers £1,500 in year 1, they can carry forward £1,500
to year 2 and have a £4,500 exemption.
• Any unused balance is lost if it is not used in the next year and that year’s exemption
must be fully used first. It cannot be carried forward to year 3.
• This exemption applies only to lifetime gifts.
B1C Small gifts
Gifts of up to £250 to any person in any one tax year are exempt.
• This exemption can be used any number of times in respect of different donees.
• The gift must be outright and cannot be a gift into trust.
• The exemption is for lifetime gifts only.
• The exemption cannot be used as part of a larger gift (for example, if a person made a
gift of £3,250 using the £3,000 annual exemption, the £250 exemption could not cover
the excess amount over the £3,000).
B1D Normal expenditure
A lifetime transfer is exempt if:
1. It was made as part of the transferor’s normal expenditure.
2. It was made out of income.
3. After allowing for all transfers forming part of normal expenditure, the transferor was left
with sufficient income to maintain their usual standard of living.
Habitual
Expenditure must be habitual or regular to count as ‘normal’.
• A good example would be a grandparent paying school fees, provided they were regular,
and it could be shown that the payments were made out of income and did not affect the
grandparent’s standard of living.
• This exemption can readily be used for payment of premiums into a life policy for the
benefit of the intended donee because the element of regularity is provided by the
premium frequency.
• The exemption cannot be used for this purpose if the transferor has also purchased an
associated annuity on their life.
• The capital content of a purchased life annuity is not treated as part of the transferor’s
income when applying this exemption to a transfer.
• If the transferor is taking withdrawals from a single premium bond, these payments are
not income under general law, even if the excess over 5% is deemed to be income for
income tax purposes.
Normal
Payments do not have to be a fixed amount to qualify as normal expenditure.
In the tax case of Bennett v. IRC (1995), a donor instructed her trustees to pay to the
donees any trust income that was surplus to her financial requirements. This resulted in two
annual payments of £9,300 and £60,000. The court held that this was normal expenditure
because the precise instructions given to the trustees established that the very different
amounts were part of a pattern of normal expenditure from income, which clearly did not
reduce her standard of living.
B1E Other gifts
Certain gifts (such as wedding gifts, gifts for education and maintenance, gifts to UK charities
and political parties, and gifts for the national benefit) plus death on active service estates
are exempt from tax.

Gifts for education and maintenance
Lifetime transfers to a spouse or civil partner for their maintenance are exempt, as are
certain payments for a child’s maintenance, education or training.
• The exemption in respect of a child only lasts until the tax year when the child becomes
18, or ends full-time education, whichever is the later.
• Illegitimate, adopted and step-children are included for this exemption, but not
grandchildren or remoter relatives.
• A transfer to a dependent relative of the donor is exempt if it constitutes reasonable
provision for the relative’s care and maintenance.
Gifts to charities and political parties
Gifts to UK charities and major national political parties are totally exempt during life and on
death, with some exceptions where the gift is not outright.
A charity must be:
• established for charitable purposes;
• registered with the Charity Commission or another regulator;
• run by fit and proper persons; and
• recognised by HMRC.
A reduced IHT rate of 36% applies to an estate in which at least 10% of the net estate is left
to a UK charity.
A political party must (at a minimum) have had at the last general election preceding the
transfer either:
• two party members elected to the House of Commons; or
• one party member elected with at least 150,000 votes being given to party candidates.
Gifts for the national benefit
Certain gifts, during lifetime or on death, for the national benefit are exempt. They include
gifts to museums, libraries, universities and the National Trust.
Gifts of land to housing associations are also exempt.
Death on active service and responding to emergency circumstances
The estates of members of the armed forces are exempt from IHT on death if they die
because of wounds received or diseases contracted on active service.
Similarly, the estates of members of the emergency services and humanitarian aid workers
who die as a result of responding to emergency circumstances are exempt from IHT, as are
the estates of police constables and armed service personnel who die as a result of being
attacked because of their status.

B2 Potentially exempt transfers
Most transfers that are not exempt are PETs.
B2A Qualifying donors and donees
A PET is a lifetime transfer by an individual to:
• another individual;
• a bare/absolute trust; or
• a disabled trust.
B2B The transfer
The consequences of making a PET are:
• No tax is charged at the date of the gift.
• The gift does not have to be reported to HMRC.
• If the donor then survives for seven years, the gift becomes fully exempt and escapes
tax entirely.
B2C Death within seven years
If the donor dies within seven years from the date of the PET, the gift becomes chargeable.
• The donee is liable to pay any tax.
• Tax is chargeable on the value of the PET at the date it was made, rather than the
possibly higher value at the date of death.
• The calculation takes into account the donor’s seven-year cumulation at the date of
the gift.
• If the PET that has become chargeable is fully within the nil rate band, the tax will be nil.
• The value of the nil rate band and the death rates of tax in force at the date of death are
used, subject to a taper relief.
Example 4.5
PET
If a donor dies one year after making a PET of a house worth £100,000 at the time of the
gift, but £110,000 at death, the value of the PET is £100,000, not £110,000.
To calculate the tax, the gift of £100,000 is added to the total of chargeable transfers in
the seven years before the date of the gift, not the seven years before death. Because of
this, events more than seven years before the death that were not PETs, but chargeable
transfers, at the time they were made can affect the amount of tax payable.
B2D Taper relief
If the donor survives for at least three years after the gift, a reduced percentage of the full
death rate is used to calculate the tax. This is called taper relief.

B2E Effect of a PET becoming chargeable
The effect of a PET becoming retrospectively chargeable is to increase the potential tax
liability on the estate. This is because the PET uses up all or part of the nil rate band. This is
the case whether or not there is any tax payable on the PET itself.
• There is no tax payable on the PET, as a result of the donor’s death, if the value of that
PET, plus any earlier chargeable transfers in the seven years ending on the date of the
PET, does not exceed the nil rate band.
• If a PET of £200,000 is made and the donor dies within seven years, there is no tax
on the PET itself, as it is within the nil rate band. The taper relief therefore provides
no benefit.
• Nevertheless, the PET has used up some, or all, of the nil rate band, and the tax on any
chargeable transfer after the PET, and on the estate at death, must take that into account.

Example 4.8
PET increases tax on the estate
If the PET is £150,000 and the death estate is £325,000, the fact that the PET has used
up £150,000 of the nil rate band means that there is only £175,000 of the nil rate band
left for the estate. This leaves £150,000 taxable at 40%, i.e. £60,000 (ignoring any RNRB).
Although the PET is not itself taxed, it increases the tax on the estate by £60,000.
If the donor had lived for seven years after the PET, there would have been no tax on
the estate as the estate would have been entirely within the nil rate band, assuming no
significant growth in its value.
Example 4.9
Bob
Bob makes a chargeable transfer of £150,000 (for example, by settling £150,000 into a
discretionary trust). Four years later, he makes a PET of £325,000. Five-and-a-half years
after the PET, he dies. The figures for the chargeable transfer and the PET are after
deducting annual exemptions.
There was no IHT to pay at the time of the chargeable transfer because it was within the
nil rate band. There was no tax to pay at the time of the PET because it was a PET.
On Bob’s death, the PET becomes chargeable. His seven-year cumulation at the time of
the PET was:
£150,000 + £325,000 = £475,000.
Using the nil rate band and death rate at the time of death, the IHT on the PET after taper
relief is:
[(£475,000 – £325,000) × 40%] − 60% = £24,000.
If there had been tax on the chargeable transfer when it was first made, that tax would
not be affected by the death because the chargeable transfer was more than seven years
before the death.
B2F Gifts into trust
Two types of gift into trust are PETs. Other gifts into trust are chargeable at the lifetime rates
when they are made unless they are otherwise exempt. For example, they are within the
annual exemption.
• A gift into a bare/absolute trust is a PET. A bare/absolute trust exists when the trustees
act as nominees for the beneficiary (or beneficiaries) who is absolutely entitled to the
assets, or would be, if aged at least 18. For most purposes, this is not a true trust, and
beneficiaries are taxed as if they owned the assets absolutely. A gift to a bare/absolute
trust is a PET because it is in effect a gift to an individual.
• The only other gifts into a trust that qualify as PETs are gifts into a disabled trust. A
disabled trust is broadly a discretionary trust in which the settled property is applied for
the benefit of the disabled person, or a trust in which a disabled person has an interest in
possession.
Refer to
See Qualifying beneficiaries and trusts on page 1/40 for the definition of a disabled
person.
B2G Relief for drop in value
If tax becomes due on a PET because the donor dies within seven years, there may be relief
where the value of the property gifted has fallen since the date of the gift.
• If the donee still owns the property, the value for the tax calculation will be the market
value on the donor’s death.
• If the donee has sold the property to an unconnected person before the donor’s death,
the value will be the market value at the date of sale.

B3 Chargeable lifetime transfers
A CLT is one that is not exempt and not potentially exempt. The most common chargeable
transfers occur on lifetime gifts to trusts other than bare/absolute trusts and trusts for a
disabled person.
• A CLT results in a tax charge if it takes the donor’s seven-year cumulation over the nil
rate band.
• Tax is payable at 20% on the excess over the nil rate band. There will be no further IHT to
pay if the donor survives for the next seven years.
• If the donor dies within seven years of making a CLT, tax at the death rates will apply
retrospectively to that transfer.
• The tax is recalculated using the value of the gift and the previous seven years’
cumulation at the date of the transfer but using the death rates of tax in force at the
date of death.
• If tax has been paid at the time of the lifetime transfer, it is allowed as a credit against the
tax payable at the death rates.
• The tax is reduced by taper relief if the donor survives more than three years after the gift.
This is the same taper relief that applies to PETs.
• Because of taper relief, the lifetime tax already paid can sometimes exceed the IHT on
death. The lifetime rate is half the death rate, but the tapered charges are 40% and
20% of the death rate in the sixth and seventh years before death, respectively. In these
cases, there will be no extra tax to pay, nor will there be a refund of tax.
• If no lifetime tax was paid because the transfer was wholly within the nil rate band, there
would usually be no extra tax to pay on death. This is because it would still be within the
nil rate band. However, tax would arise if a previous PET within the seven years before
death had retrospectively become chargeable and thereby increased the cumulation to
bring it over the IHT nil rate band. This is illustrated in the example about Dylan.
• If the value of the asset transferred has dropped between the dates of transfer and death,
relief is given on the same principles as for PETs.

B4 Transfers on death
IHT is chargeable on the death of an individual. The tax is chargeable as if the deceased had
made a transfer of value equal to the value of their estate immediately before death.
• Changes brought about by death, e.g. the payment of proceeds under a life policy, also
have to be taken into account.
• The estate is the total property to which the deceased was beneficially entitled at death,
with some exceptions, such as pension funds, which are termed ‘excluded property’.
• The value of the estate is the gross value of the assets less the debts of the individual
at death.
• A deduction is also allowed from the estate for reasonable funeral expenses.
When money is borrowed to acquire assets that qualify for business relief or agricultural
relief, or to enhance or maintain the value of such assets, then the liability reduces the value
of the assets that qualify for relief. This is the case even if the liability is actually secured
against other assets. Business relief or agricultural relief is then given against the net value
of the asset after deduction of the liability.
Example 4.14
Veronica’s estate
Veronica borrows £600,000, which is charged on her house, but used to buy shares in her
son’s company.
On death, the shares are worth £800,000 and the house is worth £1m. The liability
reduces the value of the company shares before business relief is applied, so business
relief is £200,000 (i.e. £800,000 – £600,000) and the full value of the house (£1m) is
included in the death estate.
This rule prevents Veronica obtaining the double benefit of a deductible loan against a
taxable asset and it being used to buy an asset that has 100% relief from IHT.

Be aware
Joint tenants and tenants in common
Owners can hold property either as joint tenants or as tenants in common:
• Joint tenants always own the property in equal shares – 50% each for a couple.
• Tenants in common can own the property in unequal shares if they wish.
In both cases, the deceased’s share of the property is included in their estate for IHT
purposes (although it will still be exempt if passing to a spouse or civil partner). However,
the type of tenancy affects how the property passes:
• With a joint tenancy, on first death, the property automatically passes to the survivor.
• If property is owned as tenants in common, the deceased owns a set share of the
property, and this share passes according to the terms of their will (or under the
intestacy rules if there is no will).
B4A Excluded property
Excluded property is ignored in valuing a person’s estate at death. The most common
forms are:
• pension funds:
– In addition to usually being free of IHT, there is no charge to income tax if the
deceased was under age 75 (this is subject to funds being taken as an annuity or
drawdown or, if taken as a lump sum, the lump sum and death benefit allowance
(LSDBA) of £1,073,100 is not exceeded).
– If pension funds are inherited on or after age 75, then subsequent pension
withdrawals are taxed on the beneficiary as income and subject to the normal rates of
income tax (any funds in excess of the LSDBA, if the deceased was under age 75, are
taxed on a similar basis).
– From 6 April 2027, most unused pension funds will become subject to IHT and
included in a deceased’s estate, unless inherited by a spouse or civil partner (as such
transfers are exempt).
• property situated outside the UK where the owner is not long-term resident in the UK;
• reversionary interests in trusts unless the interest has been acquired for money or
money’s worth:
– A reversionary interest is a right to capital when the current beneficiary’s right to
income under the trust comes to an end – an example would be the remainderman’s
interest in a life interest trust.
• holdings in authorised unit trusts and shares in open-ended investment companies
(OEICs) when the beneficial owner is not long-term resident in the UK.

B4C Reduced rate
A reduced IHT rate of 36% applies to an estate in which at least 10% of the net estate is left
to a registered UK charity.
The net estate is that remaining after deducting exemptions (but excluding the charitable
legacy), reliefs and the nil rate band (but not the RNRB). The reduced rate of 36% is
automatic without any need for the personal representatives to make a claim.
If the estate includes components consisting of property that passes by survivorship (usually
property jointly owned with another person) and/or settled property in which the deceased
was beneficially entitled to an interest in possession, the 10% calculation is carried out
separately for each component. For this purpose, the nil rate band is divided proportionately
between the components by reference to their value. Beneficiaries can elect to merge the
components for the purposes of the 10% calculation.

B4E Individuals dying simultaneously
When two individuals die in circumstances in which it is impossible to say who died first, the
general law presumes that the older one was the first to die (Law of Property Act 1925,
s.184). Thus, if the older person left the property to the younger one, two successive tax
charges could occur for that property. For IHT purposes, it is presumed that they died at the
same instant, thus avoiding the double charge.
If a beneficiary dies within a short time (six months or less) of a testator, a double charge can
be avoided by a provision in the will making the gift effective only if the beneficiary survives a
set period.
B4F Transfer of nil rate band
Any unused nil rate band on a person’s death can be transferred to the estate of their
surviving spouse or registered civil partner. This must be claimed on the second death.
• HMRC publishes a list of nil rate bands from 1914 onwards, so it is possible to transfer
the unused nil rate band from a spouse who died before the introduction of IHT (their
estate would have been liable to estate duty or capital transfer tax).
• The couple must have been married or in a civil partnership at the time of the first death.
• The transfer of the nil rate band is not affected by the survivor remarrying or entering a
new civil partnership.
• The unused nil rate band on the first death is the amount that is not used against the
estate, or by any chargeable transfers in the seven years before death (this includes
PETs that became chargeable as a result of the death).
• The amount available on the second death is based on the current rate of nil rate band
and is calculated as the proportion of the nil rate band not used on the first death.
• The transferred nil rate band cannot be used against chargeable lifetime transfers.
However, it can be used against any additional tax due on a lifetime transfer that arises
as a result of the second death.

There is an upper limit of 100% by which the nil rate band may be increased. In other words,

two circumstances, which may occur separately or together:
1. The first spouse or civil partner to die was entitled to the nil rate band transferred from a
previous partner.
2. The second to die is entitled to the nil rate band transferred from more than one
previous partner.

A relief reduces the value of a transfer in certain circumstances. In theory, it does not remove

• business relief;
• agricultural relief; and

on death.

• a business or an interest in a business;
• shares in an unlisted company;
• controlling shares in a listed company; or
• land, buildings or machinery owned by the deceased and used in their business.

C1A Non-qualifying assets
Certain business assets do not qualify for business relief or may reduce the amount of

shareholders obliged to buy, the shares on the death of their owner. This is a common
situation under share purchase agreements of small companies.
• Certain assets must be disregarded when valuing business property, such as shares or
an interest in a business:
– any asset that has not been used in the business during the previous two years; and
– assets not required for future use in the business at the time of the transfer.

large amounts of cash or investments that are not genuine business assets.

business. The cash must be required for a specific business purpose; it is not enough

relief.
The value of Walter’s estate for IHT purposes is therefore:
£175,000 + £30,000 + £8,000 + £50,200 + £49,000 + £0 (i.e. £80,000 – £80,000) +
£246,000 + £250,000 = £808,200.
C2 Agricultural relief
Agricultural relief is a relief for transfers of agricultural property situated in the UK. Relief is
available for lifetime transfers and on death.
• Agricultural property includes agricultural land, growing crops and farm buildings, but not
the animals or equipment.
• The relief is given on the agricultural value of the land, but not on any development value.
Agricultural property does not include buildings on their own.
• Relief is not given on any excess of the open market value of a farmhouse over its
agricultural value. Relief may be denied entirely if the occupier of the farmhouse is not
involved in farming the land on a day-to-day basis.
• The relief is:
– 100% (subject to the 100% relief allowance – see 100% relief allowance on page
11/17 for further information) for owner-occupied farms and farm tenancies; and
– 50% for interests of landlords in let farmland, and assets qualifying for 100% relief, but
where the 100% relief allowance has been exceeded.
• The 50% relief is increased to 100% for land let under tenancies exceeding twelve
months that started after 31 August 1995.
• The property must have been:
– occupied by the transferor for agricultural purposes for the previous two years; or
– owned by the transferor for seven years and occupied by someone else for
agricultural purposes for that time.
• Agricultural relief does not apply to property that is subject to a binding contract for sale.

• When agricultural relief and business relief are both available, agricultural relief is
given first.
– A farm with property development value will qualify for agricultural relief on its
agricultural value and business relief may be given on the enhanced value.
– Generally, assets in a farming business will qualify for business relief, even if they do
not qualify for agricultural relief.
• If the donor dies within seven years of making a gift that qualified for agricultural relief,
there may be a tax liability. Examples are if the land is not still owned by the recipient or if
it no longer qualifies as agricultural land.

Nigel
Nigel died on 20 December 2026. Included in his estate are:
• agricultural land valued at £400,000, but with a development value of £740,000;
• farm equipment valued at £70,000; and
• a farmhouse with an agricultural value of £230,000, but an open market value of
£340,000. This property was occupied by Nigel for agricultural purposes for the ten
years up to his death.
Agricultural relief will be given on the agricultural value of the land and the farmhouse, so
relief will be: £400,000 + £230,000 = £630,000.
Provided the farm was being run as a trade, business relief should be available on the
value of the farm equipment and the difference between the development value of the
land and the agricultural value. Relief will therefore be: £70,000 + (£740,000 – £400,000)
= £410,000.
The difference between the open market value of the farmhouse and its agricultural
value would not normally qualify for business relief given that the farmhouse is a
residential property.
C3 100% relief allowance
There is a limit of £2.5m, known as the 100% relief allowance, on the combined amount of
business property and agricultural property that can qualify for 100% IHT relief. For assets in
excess of £2.5m, relief is given only at the rate of 50%.
• If the total value of business property and agricultural property to which 100% relief
applies is more than £2.5m, the 100% relief allowance is applied proportionately across
the qualifying property. For example, if there is business property of £4m and agricultural
property of £6m, the business property makes up 40% of the value and agricultural
property 60%; therefore, the 100% allowance for the business property will be £1m, and
for the agricultural property, it will be £1.5m.
• Assets that automatically receive 50% business relief or agricultural relief do not use up
the 100% relief allowance.
• When used against lifetime transfers, the 100% relief allowance is refreshed every seven
years. However, the allowance is only applied to lifetime transfers made on or after 30
October 2024. Any 100% business relief or agricultural relief given on lifetime transfers
made before 30 October 2024 does not use up the 100% relief allowance.
• Any unused 100% relief allowance on a person’s death can be transferred to the estate
of their surviving spouse or registered civil partner (even if first death occurred before 6
April 2026; the date from which the 100% relief allowance was introduced). This must
be claimed on the second death. The 100% relief allowance is transferrable, even if the
business property or agricultural property is owned solely by the surviving spouse or
registered civil partner.

C4 Woodlands relief
Woodlands relief is a special relief for growing timber situated in the UK.
• The relief applies only to the timber and not the land itself, which may, in any case, qualify

been felled.
• The relief applies to transfers on death, not lifetime gifts, and it operates by deferring the
tax until disposal of the timber.
• However, the occupation of woodlands for commercial purposes would mean that
business relief could be claimed at 100%, which is preferable to deferment.

D Gifts with reservation
Property that a donor has given away is treated as still belonging to the donor for IHT
purposes if the donor continues to enjoy a benefit from the property.
D1 What is a gift with reservation?
A gift is treated as a gift with a reservation of benefit if it is not enjoyed to the exclusion or
virtual exclusion of the donor.
Examples of gifts with reservation are a donor giving away:
• a house, but continuing to live in it without paying full market rent;
• a painting, but leaving it on their own sitting room wall;
• a sum of money, but continuing to receive interest on it; and
• a freehold interest in land but the donor retains a lease, which was granted or acquired
less than seven years before the gift.
Chapter 4
Chapter 4 Inheritance tax (IHT) 4/25
===PAGE 157===
The most common example of a gift with reservation is the use of a house and whether
or not rent is paid can be particularly important. If a parent gives a house to their child but
continues to live in the house rent-free, that will be a gift with reservation.
If, at some later time, the parent starts to pay a market rent for living in the house, the
reservation will cease at that point. The gift becomes an outright gift and will be exempt if
the parent lives for a further seven years. Conversely, there may initially be no reservation
because market rent is paid – but at a later point, the rent ceases or falls below a market
rent. A reservation will then occur at that point.
D2 Consequences of a gift with reservation
If the donor still retains a benefit in the gift at death, its value is treated as remaining in the
donor’s estate and is taxed accordingly.
• The gift is nevertheless a transfer of value at the time of the gift, which may be
chargeable or become chargeable as a result of the donor’s death within seven years.
• The result may be a double IHT charge.
– The donor will be treated as making a second gift when they cease to retain their
interest in the gift (e.g. move out of the house, physically give away the painting or
stop taking the interest from the gift).
– If the donor dies, the gift with reservation will be added back into the estate on the
basis of its value at death.
• There is relief when a double charge to IHT arises – on the original gift and at death.
Generally, if tax has already been paid on the original gift, it is offset against the tax
payable at death. However, this provision does not prevent the original gift using up all
or part of the nil rate band if the original gift was made within the seven years before
the death.
• The gift with reservation rules only affect IHT. Such a gift is valid for other purposes, e.g.
CGT, and the donee is the new legal owner.
Gifts with reservation should generally be avoided.
Example 4.23
Frank
Frank made a gift of a country cottage to his son but continued to use it several months a
year without paying his son any rent. The value was £160,000 when he made the gift.
When Frank died, the cottage was worth £340,000, which was the amount that was
included in his estate at death for IHT purposes.
• The gift was a disposal for CGT purposes.
• If the cottage had stayed in Frank’s ownership, any CGT liability would have been
effectively removed.
• If Frank had made a gift without reservation when it was worth only £160,000, the
estate would have been saved IHT on the growth in value of £180,000 or on the whole
£340,000 if the gift were more than seven years before the death.
D3 Trusts
HMRC has stated that if the donor is a potential beneficiary under a discretionary trust,
or even under a power of appointment trust in which someone else has an interest in
possession, it regards this as a reservation of benefit.
• The standard advice for clients using these trusts is to make sure that they cannot
themselves benefit in any way by excluding themselves from benefit in the trust.
• The donor can still be a trustee, but they should not be able to benefit from any trustee
charging clause.

D4 Exclusion from reservation rule
The gift with reservation provisions do not apply to gifts that are exempt because they are:
• transfers between spouses or civil partners;
• small gifts;
• gifts in consideration of marriage or civil partnership;
• gifts to charity;
• gifts to political parties; and
• gifts for the national benefit.
A benefit can be reserved for the spouse or civil partner of the donor, but if that spouse
or civil partner shares enjoyment of the gift with the donor, HMRC may regard this as a
reservation of benefit by the donor.
HMRC does not regard an interest-free loan on its own (that is, without any other
arrangements between the borrower and lender) as a gift with reservation, provided it is
repayable on demand or at the latest on the death of the lender. The lender’s ability to
demand immediate repayment ensures that the loan does not reduce the lender’s estate.
HMRC has stated that where a gift is made into trust, the retention by the settlor of a
reversionary interest under the trust is not a reservation of benefit.

E Pre-owned assets tax
Various schemes enabled people to avoid the gift with reservation provisions and to
effectively pass on their home to their children while continuing to live in it. The spread
of such schemes and the uncertainty over whether HMRC could successfully fight them
using the legislation then in existence prompted the Government to introduce an income tax
charge on certain IHT planning arrangements. This POAT charge came into effect on 6 April
2005 but can apply to arrangements put in place many years earlier.
E1 The charge to pre-owned assets tax
Income tax is charged on the benefit people get by having free or low-cost enjoyment or use
of certain assets they formerly owned or provided the funds to purchase. The rules quantify
an annual cash value for the benefit, and this is treated as an addition to their taxable
income in each year in which they enjoy the benefit.
Chapter 4
Chapter 4 Inheritance tax (IHT) 4/27
===PAGE 159===
• Assets that may be caught by POAT are:
– land, including living accommodation;
– chattels, e.g. pictures and antiques; and
– intangible assets comprised in a settlement in which the settlor retains an interest. In
practice, this mainly covers life assurance policies and has implications for some IHT
planning arrangements.
• In the case of land, the cash value of the benefit is based on market rentals.
• For chattels and intangible assets, the cash value will usually be a percentage of the
capital value, the percentage being equal to the official interest rate for income tax
purposes. The official interest rate was set at 3.75% from 6 April 2026, but will be
reviewed on a quarterly basis throughout 2026/27.
• No tax is charged in any year in which the cash value of benefits is £5,000 or less.
• The valuation date for property subject to the charge is:
– 6 April in the relevant tax year; or
– the date it becomes subject to the charge if that is later.
• If the property is land or chattels, it must be revalued every five years.
• If a person pays for the benefit obtained from the asset, the amount paid is deducted from
the taxable cash value. Of course, there is no POAT where the person pays full market
value for the use of the asset because there is no benefit.
E2 Arrangements subject to pre-owned assets tax
Disposals of assets any time from 18 March 1986 onwards (the date of introduction of IHT)
will give rise to POAT if the former owner enjoys a benefit under these rules. Therefore, it
is necessary to examine all IHT plans put in place since 18 March 1986 and still in effect to
determine whether they are caught by POAT. The charge applies to UK residents.
• For long-term residents in the UK, the charge applies to worldwide assets.
• For people who are not long-term resident in the UK, the charge applies only to
UK assets.
• For people who have become long-term resident in the UK, the charge does not apply to
any non-UK assets that they ceased to own before becoming long-term resident.
E3 Exclusions and exemptions
Certain disposals are excluded from the POAT charges on land and chattels. The concept of
excluded transactions has no application to intangible property. The main ones are:
• assets transferred to a spouse or civil partner;
• assets in which the former owner, spouse or civil partner has an interest in possession;
and
• assets sold at an arm’s length price paid in cash:
– Sales to connected persons are within this exclusion if the price is similar to that which
an unconnected purchaser would have paid.
– Sales in which the consideration is left outstanding as a debt are not within it.
Certain property is exempt from POAT on land, chattels and intangible property, including:
• assets that the former owner ceased to own before 18 March 1986;
• assets that count as part of the taxpayer’s estate under the gifts with reservation rules;
• if the taxpayer was formerly the owner of an asset only by virtue of a will or intestacy that
has subsequently been varied by agreement between the beneficiaries; and
• if any enjoyment of the asset is no more than incidental: this includes cases in which an
outright gift to a family member comes to benefit the donor following a change in their
circumstances, such as ill health or disability.
In general, former owners will not be regarded as enjoying a taxable benefit if they retain an
interest that is consistent with their ongoing enjoyment of the property. For example, there is
no income tax charge if a person who formerly owned the whole of their home passes a 50%
interest to someone who lives with them and pays their share of the running costs.

HMRC has said that the income tax charge will not apply to a taxpayer who has funded life
assurance policies held on trust if:
• the taxpayer’s continuing claims are limited to particular retained benefits, such as the

• the balance of the policy value is held on trust solely for others.
But income tax would be charged if, for example, the whole value of such a life policy
was held on discretionary trusts for a class of beneficiaries including the settlor (and the
circumstances were such that the trust property was not covered by the existing ‘gift with
reservation’ rules).
• This may catch some business life assurance trusts, set up to ensure that a partnership
or private company can continue after the death of one of the partners or shareholders
while enabling the estate of the deceased to receive the value of the interest in the
business.
• In many cases, the value of the benefit will not exceed £5,000, so no tax will be
chargeable.
The Government has also said that all commercial equity release schemes are exempt from
the tax, even when the owner has not disposed of the whole of the property.
E4 Avoiding pre-owned assets tax
Individuals whose arrangements are caught by POAT might be able to undo them. However,
this may not be possible with some trust-based plans especially if any of the beneficiaries of
a trust are minors.
Another way of avoiding the tax is by electing for the asset to be subject to IHT on death.
• This is done by completing form IHT500.
• The normal deadline for the election is 31 January following the end of the first tax year in
which income tax arises. If income tax first arises in 2026/27, the election should usually
be made by 31 January 2028.
• HMRC has the discretion to accept late elections without restriction.
• Regulations generally prevent a double charge to IHT on a death within seven years of
the original gift. Without special rules, IHT would have been payable twice: on the PET
becoming chargeable and again on the asset that is the subject of the election.
There are several factors to consider in deciding whether to opt out of the POAT. In some
cases, it may be preferable to suffer some income tax in order to achieve an IHT saving.

F Valuation of the transfer
The value of an asset for IHT purposes is generally taken as the price it might reasonably be
expected to fetch if it were sold on the open market. No reduction in this value is allowed for
the fact that if the whole property, e.g. a large shareholding, were put on the market at one
time, this would reduce the price.
• Any expenses of the transfer, e.g. legal costs, are disregarded if they are paid by the
transferor, but they reduce the value of the transfer if they are paid by the transferee.
• Where a chargeable transfer also results in a CGT liability, which is paid by the
transferee, the value for IHT purposes is reduced by the amount of the CGT paid.
• If a deceased’s estate includes listed shares or authorised unit trust holdings, which are
sold within a year of the death at a lower value than the value at the date of death,
relief is given by recalculating the tax on the basis of the actual sale value of all the
sold shares.
• A similar provision applies to sales of land to unconnected persons within three,
sometimes four, years of the death, but only if the loss is more than the lower of £1,000,
or 5% of the death value.

F1 Related property
Related property is property that is either in the estate of a spouse or civil partner, or in a
settlement in which the donor or spouse or civil partner has an interest in possession.
• When donors transfer related property, they are deemed to have transferred the value of
their proportion of the total value of all the related property.
• For example, if a husband and wife each own 45% of the shares in a private company,
and the wife gives all her shares to her son, she is deemed to have transferred half
the value of a 90% controlling shareholding rather than the value of a 45% minority
shareholding.
• A controlling shareholding is worth more than a minority shareholding and therefore, this
rule usually increases the value of the transfer.
F2 Connected persons
HMRC looks carefully at the valuations of transfers between connected parties, even if they
are claimed to be commercial transactions, to ensure that there is no element of gift.
F3 Valuation of life policies
When the life assured dies with an own life policy, not assigned or under trust, the death sum
assured is part of the estate and so, is potentially taxable.
• If a life policy is the subject of a transfer of value during its lifetime, the value based on
normal IHT rules is its market value, usually the surrender value, unless the life assured
is known to be in very bad health.
• A special valuation rule exists for life policies. Under this rule, the value is not less than
the total premiums paid under the policy minus any sums previously paid out. The normal
market value (surrender value) is used if it is higher.
• The special premium valuation rule does not apply to term assurances for three years or
less, or if the term exceeds three years, where:
– premiums are payable for at least two-thirds of the term; and
– premiums payable in any one year do not exceed twice those in any other year.
• Unit-linked policies are not fully subject to the premiums valuation, because any drop
in the value of units since they were allocated is allowed as a deduction from the
total premiums.
• If premiums are paid on a life policy for the benefit of someone other than the payer, there
is a transfer of value of the amount of the premium.
F4 Grossing up
CLTs have to be grossed up where the transferor pays the tax. This rule stems from the
principle that the tax is based on the loss to the transferor’s estate.
• If the transferor pays the tax, the estate is reduced by both the value of the asset
transferred and the IHT on it.
• Tax is calculated on the total of the gift plus the tax.
• It is therefore necessary to gross up the net transfer to calculate the tax.

Example 4.24
Maryam
Maryam decides to make a gross gift of £10,000 and the relevant rate is 20%. The tax is

However, if Maryam wishes the donee to receive £10,000 net of tax, she has to make a

It is important to understand this principle, as it is the grossed-up value that is
accumulated for future transfers. There is no grossing up if the transferee pays the tax
because the loss to the transferor is merely the value of the asset. It can only apply to
lifetime transfers and, of course, only to the excess over the nil rate band.
The tax payable when transfers are grossed up is one-quarter of the excess over the nil
rate band.

G Liability to inheritance tax
The provisions for payment of IHT depend on whether the tax arises on a lifetime transfer or
on death.
G1 Lifetime transfers
IHT on a CLT is primarily charged on the transferor, but it can also be paid by the transferee.
• The amount of tax payable is affected by who pays the tax due to the grossing up rule.
• Tax is usually due six months after the end of the month when the transfer was made; but
for a transfer made after 5 April and before 1 October in any year, the tax is due on 30
April in the following year.
• If a transferor dies within seven years of a PET or CLT, any extra tax due is payable by
the transferee six months after the end of the month in which death occurs.
• When a person makes a CLT and IHT is payable, the transferor must report this to HMRC
so that the tax can be assessed.
• When a transferor has made a PET or a gift with reservation and tax becomes payable
because of the transferor’s death, the transferee must report this to HMRC. The donor’s
personal representatives must also report such gifts.

G2 Transfers on death
Tax payable on the estate of a deceased person is the liability of the legal personal
representatives (LPRs). Tax is due six months after the end of the month in which
death occurs.
• The LPRs cannot get their grant of representation to administer the estate until they have
delivered an account of the deceased’s assets and paid the tax.
• Therefore, in practice, tax is often paid before the due date.
• Some banks and building societies are able to accept instructions from legal personal
representatives to pay IHT by electronic transfer out of the deceased’s accounts. If this
is not possible, legal personal representatives may have to take a loan in order to pay
the tax because they do not have access to the deceased’s assets until the tax has
been paid.
• There are provisions for paying tax in instalments on certain property, such as land.
• If a testator divides their estate between an exempt beneficiary (e.g. a charity) and a
non-exempt beneficiary (e.g. a child), the estate is divided before the calculation of the
tax so that the burden of tax is on the non-exempt beneficiary.
Example 4.26
Joe
Joe dies leaving an estate of £1m, half to his daughter Freda, and half to his favourite
charity. The estate does not include a residence.
• The charity’s half is exempt, so it receives £500,000.
• Freda’s half is not exempt, so her £500,000 is taxable:
– £325,000 at 0% = £0; and
– £175,000 at 36% = £63,000.
Therefore, she receives: £500,000 – £63,000 = £437,000.
Since half of the estate has been left to charity, the 10% charitable legacy limit has clearly
been met.

H Inheritance tax and trusts
H1 The inheritance tax liability of trustees
The creation of a trust is a transfer of value for IHT purposes. The amount of the transfer is
based on the loss in value to the settlor’s estate.
Chapter 4
4/32 R03/July 2026 Personal taxation
===PAGE 164===
Tax may be payable and this will depend on:
• whether the settlor’s nil rate band (£325,000 in 2026/27) has been exceeded;
• the terms of the trust;
• what exemptions are available and the type of trust that is used; and
• whether the settlor has made any other CLTs in the previous seven years.
There is no IHT liability if an exemption can be claimed. Examples include:
• a gift into trust within any available annual £3,000 exemptions;
• a gift to a charitable trust; or
• a transfer that qualified for business relief or agricultural relief.
A transfer to a bare/absolute trust is treated as a PET. If the transfer into a trust is not fully or
potentially exempt, there could be an IHT charge.
Relevant property trusts
The IHT treatment of trusts was radically changed by the Finance Act 2006, and most
lifetime trusts created since the changes (from 22 March 2006) are subject to the relevant
property trust rules.
H2 Bare/absolute trusts
A transfer of value to a bare/absolute trust is a PET. The trust fund will be in the IHT estate of
the beneficiary.
If the settlor:
• survives seven complete years, the PET will not be chargeable to IHT; or
• does not survive seven complete years, the PET will have failed and will be liable to IHT
to the extent that it is not covered by any available nil rate band.
However, if the settlor has survived three or more years, the tax charge may be reduced by
the application of taper relief. The beneficiary of a bare/absolute trust will be liable for any
IHT payable on a failed PET.
H3 Trusts for disabled beneficiaries and vulnerable
persons
The creation of a trust for a disabled or vulnerable person is a PET, even if it is a
discretionary trust.
• Such a trust can only be taxable if the settlor dies within seven years; moreover, there are
no exit or periodic charges.
• The definition of a disabled person for this purpose is the same as under the income tax
and CGT legislation for trusts for vulnerable beneficiaries.
• The Finance Act 2006 also introduced a provision allowing a person to settle their own
property on themselves (i.e. a self-interest trust) without an IHT charge if they could show
they have a condition that will lead to a disability in the future.
H4 Life interest and flexible interest in possession trusts
H4A Pre-22 March 2006
For trusts established before 22 March 2006, the creation during the settlor’s lifetime was
a PET. There would only be a tax charge if the settlor died in the following seven years, in
which case the trustees would primarily be liable for the IHT.
• The trust assets are generally regarded as being owned by the beneficiary with the
interest in possession and are therefore in their taxable estate for IHT purposes.
• If there are two equal interest-in-possession beneficiaries, they each have an interest
in possession in half the trust fund. Similarly, three equal interest-in-possession
beneficiaries will have an interest-in-possession in one-third of the trust fund and so on.
However, if the beneficiary with the interest in possession is changed after 5 October
2008, then the assets are usually not treated as part of their estate unless the change
occurred as a result of the death of the new beneficiary’s spouse or civil partner.

H4B Changes in ownership of interest in possession trusts created

In relation to pre-22 March 2006 interest in possession trusts, in which the interest in

This would happen on:
• the death of an absolute beneficiary;
• the death of a life tenant; or
• an appointment under a flexible trust.
The tax charge is calculated using the outgoing beneficiary’s rates and exemptions but the
proportion of tax relating to the trust property is payable by the trustees out of the trust fund.

A change in the interest in possession in a pre-22 March 2006 trust during the lifetime of the
outgoing beneficiary (e.g. by the trustees using their power of appointment) is deemed to be

outgoing beneficiary. An absolute appointment in favour of another beneficiary will be treated
as a PET from the outgoing beneficiary.
So, in either a pre- or post-6 October 2008 case, the IHT impact for the outgoing beneficiary

Special rules apply to life policies placed under interest in possession trusts before
22 March 2006.

H4C Post-22 March 2006
The creation of a lifetime trust with an interest in possession is usually a CLT when the trust
was established on or after 22 March 2006.
When there is a life interest (an entitlement to income for life), the life tenant has the interest
in possession. Under a flexible trust, the ‘gift over’ (i.e. default) beneficiary has the interest
until an appointment is made. The trust is subject to entry, exit and periodic charges. There
are two exceptions to this; these charges do not apply to:
1. trusts for disabled or vulnerable persons; or
2. IPDI trusts created on death by will or intestacy.
Immediate post-death interest
The Finance Act 2006 defined an IPDI trust as one where a person has an interest in
possession in settled property and both of the following conditions apply:
1. The settlement was effected by will or under intestacy.
2. The beneficiary became beneficially entitled to the interest in possession on the death of
the testator or intestate.
The trust would commonly be set up for the benefit of any surviving spouse so, as such,
there would be no IHT to pay on the death of the first spouse. In addition, under current IHT
laws, the first spouse’s nil rate band would remain intact and could be used on the death of
the second spouse.
H5 Discretionary trusts and other relevant property trusts
created by lifetime gifts on or after 22 March 2006
The IHT rules have always been different for a trust that has no interest in possession, i.e.
a discretionary trust. However, the discretionary trust rules also apply to most other trusts
created in lifetime and on or after 22 March 2006.
Legislation on same-day additions was included in the Finance (No.2) Act 2015. If property
is added to a number of settlements on the same day, the value of the addition to all the
settlements concerned and the initial value of relevant property settled are added together.
The result is then taken into account in arriving at the hypothetical cumulative total when
determining the rate of tax to be charged on the other settlements.
H5A Creation of the trust
The creation of the trust is a CLT and tax will be payable at 20% if the transfer into trust
takes the settlor’s cumulative total of CLTs in the previous seven years over the nil rate band
(£325,000 in 2026/27).
There may also be further tax to pay if the settlor dies within seven years. Taper relief will be
available to reduce any IHT payable when:
• the transfer is in excess of any available nil rate band; and
• the settlor survived at least three years from the date of the gift.
If a beneficiary dies, there is no value to add to the beneficiary’s estate for being a
beneficiary of either a discretionary trust or an interest in possession trust created on or
after 22 March 2006.
H5B Exit charges
IHT is chargeable each time a capital distribution or appointment of capital is made
to a beneficiary.
• When capital (either part or all) is distributed or appointed during the first ten years, tax
is chargeable on the basis of 30% of the lifetime rate that would have been charged on a
hypothetical transfer by the settlor using their cumulative total of gifts in the seven years
prior to creating the trust.
• However, only x/40 of the full tax is charged: x is the number of full three-month periods
(quarter years) for which the property has been held on a discretionary trust during the
initial ten-year period.

• A similar method is used to calculate an exit charge that arises after the first ten years,
except that the rate used is the last periodic charge rate, i.e. the rate charged on the last
tenth anniversary of the trust, although the x/40 rule still applies.
• There is no exit charge on any payment that is treated as income for income
tax purposes.
When trustees decide to distribute all of the trust capital, this will bring the trust to an end
and give rise to a potential exit charge for IHT purposes.
H5C Periodic (ten-year anniversary) charges
As well as a tax charge on distributions, there is also a potential periodic tax charge on the
value of the trust fund whether or not distributions have been made.
• The periodic charge occurs on each tenth anniversary of the date of the creation of
the trust.
• The charge is at 30% of the lifetime rate (currently 20%) that would have applied to
a hypothetical transfer by the settlor. This makes a maximum charge of 6% of the
trust fund.
• The settlor’s seven-year cumulation is taken immediately before the creation of the trust
(i.e. the trustees ‘inherit’ the settlor’s available nil rate band) plus the value of any capital
appointments or distributions made in the previous ten years.
The maximum rate of tax during the life of a trust is therefore 6% (i.e. 30% × 20%) every
ten years. Although this may result in no tax liability, the calculation can often be very
complicated.
Accumulated and undistributed income in a discretionary trust
If the trustees decide to accumulate income within the trust rather than distribute it, this
retained income loses its character as income and is thereafter treated as capital. The
accumulated income will then count as relevant property for the purposes of the IHT
periodic charge.
The accumulated income becomes relevant property on the date the decision to accumulate
is made. This means that there is an adjustment to the effective rate of tax to reflect the fact
that the accumulated income has not been in the trust for the full 40 quarters.
Since April 2014, retained income that has not been formally accumulated, but that arises
more than five years before the ten-year anniversary, is treated as part of the capital value of
the trust and is subject to the periodic charge (IHTA 1984).
This deemed accumulated income will be subject to the full ten-year charge. In the interests
of simplifying the calculations, there will be no proportionate reduction for the period before
the income arose. But if the same income was formally accumulated, the rate would be
adjusted (IHTA 1984).
Example 4.28
Creating the trust
Stan sets up a discretionary trust in June 2026 for his grandchildren for £425,000. This
is a CLT.
Stan had a full nil rate band available, but he had already used up his annual exemptions
to date. Using the current rates for 2026/27, the IHT payable on setting up the trust is:
£425,000 – £325,000 = £100,000
£100,000 × 20% = £20,000
If the trustees paid the tax, this is the amount of the liability.
If Stan paid the tax, the transfer would have been grossed up and the tax would be:
£100,000 × (100 ÷ 80) × 20% = £25,000.
The reason the tax is higher if paid by the settlor, rather than if paid by the trustees, is that
the IHT charge is based on the total loss to the settlor’s estate. In this example, the total
loss to Stan’s estate is the cost of the gift plus the cost of the tax. The grossing-up of the
gift enables tax due to be calculated on the total (gross) gift.

H6 Trusts for minors
The Finance Act 2006 introduced new rules for two types of trust created on the death of
a parent. These allow income to be accumulated and do not apply the full rigours of the
periodic and exit charge rules.
H6A Trusts for bereaved minors
Trusts for bereaved minors may be created on the death of a parent by their will or intestacy,
or under the Criminal Injuries Compensation Scheme.
These trusts must give an absolute interest (in the income and capital of the trust) at age 18.
Until that age, the trust assets are treated as the child’s for IHT purposes (i.e. as applies to
pre-22 March 2006 interest in possession trusts). Thereafter, the assets transferred to them
absolutely will also form a part of their estate for IHT purposes.
There are no periodic charges or exit charges provided the income and capital of the trust go
to the beneficiary at age 18.
H6B 18 to 25 trusts
These trusts may be created for minors on the death of a parent by their will or intestacy, or
under the Criminal Injuries Compensation Scheme. An absolute interest must be given to a
specified beneficiary no later than age 25.
The beneficiary is only treated as owning the trust assets for IHT purposes until they reach
age 18. An exit charge is levied when the absolute entitlement is given, based on the period
since the beneficiary’s 18th birthday.
Example 4.30
18 to 25 trust for Peter
An 18 to 25 trust was created on the death of Sally for her son, Peter. The absolute
interest is given to Peter on his 25th birthday. The value of the trust assets at that time is
£400,000 and the IHT nil rate band is £325,000.
The exit charge is based on the number of quarter years between Peter’s 18th birthday
and when he becomes absolutely entitled, i.e. 28/40ths, and is levied on the trust value at
the ‘effective rate’.
First, we need to calculate the effective rate to calculate the periodic charge:
(£400,000 − £325,000) × 30% × 20%
= £75,000 × 30% × 20% = £4,500.
This is an effective rate of 1.125% (i.e. £4,500 ÷ £400,000 × 100 = 1.125).
The exit charge is therefore:
£400,000 × 28 ÷ 40 × 1.125% = £3,150.

H7 Accumulation and maintenance trusts
Accumulation and maintenance (A&M) trusts were a special type of discretionary
trust, governed by the IHTA 1984, and exempt from the periodic and exit charge
taxation provisions.
As a result of the Finance Act 2006, since 22 March 2006, it has not been possible to create
such tax-favoured trusts. The pre-22 March 2006 rules only continued to apply to existing
A&M trusts until 5 April 2008.

• An A&M trust gave no immediate interest in possession, but one or more beneficiaries
had to acquire an absolute interest or an interest in possession on or before a specified
age not exceeding 25.
• In the meantime, any income could be either accumulated or applied for the
maintenance, education or benefit of a beneficiary.
• A&M trusts were not subject to the periodic tax charge and were not taxed when capital
was distributed or when a beneficiary attained the specified age.
• The creation of an A&M trust by a lifetime transfer was a PET. Therefore, it became
chargeable only if the settlor died within seven years of setting up the trust.
This treatment applied only to a trust that had been in existence for less than 25 years or
when all the beneficiaries were grandchildren of a common grandparent (or their widows,
widowers or children).
The IHT advantages of A&M trusts made them a good vehicle for grandparents to make gifts
to grandchildren. This enabled wealth to be passed to them free of IHT, provided the settlor
survived seven years, and was used to pay school fees or otherwise for the benefit of the
beneficiaries. The IHT consequences of setting up such a trust were the same as for an
absolute gift, but using a trust enabled the trustees to retain control of the capital and gave
them discretion over the distribution of income.
The changes introduced in the Finance Act 2006 mean that the IHT treatment for A&M trusts
ended on 6 April 2008 if no changes had been made beforehand.
The treatment is based on the three options open to the trustees before 6 April 2008:
1. If income and capital are transferred to the beneficiary at age 18
If the original trust deed was such, or the trust deed was amended so that the beneficiaries
become absolutely entitled to the income and capital of the trust at age 18, then there
are no periodic or exit charges. This is the only way the existing A&M trust can retain its
IHT advantages.
2. If capital passes to the beneficiary at age 25 (whether income at 18 or not)
• If the trust was amended to mirror an 18 to 25 trust or already provided for absolute
entitlement at age 25, then there is an exit charge when the capital entitlement arises, but
no periodic charge.
• The exit charge is based on the number of quarters arising after the 18th birthday until
the capital entitlement.
• If the capital does not pass until the beneficiary is 25, the exit charge will be charged for
seven years (25 less 18 years).
3. If the trust is unchanged and capital passes to the beneficiary after the age of 25
• If the trust did not provide for absolute entitlement at age 25 and has been left
unchanged, it is treated as a relevant property trust (like a discretionary trust) from
6 April 2008.
• Ten-year periodic charges and exit charges may be payable after 5 April 2008.
• The ten-yearly anniversaries will arise by reference to the original date of the settlement
and calculated as noted earlier. The charge will then be based on the period from 6 April
2008 to the ten-year anniversary of the original date of the settlement, based on the
number of complete quarters.

Key points
The main ideas covered by this chapter can be summarised as follows:
Basic principles of inheritance tax
• The legislation for inheritance tax (IHT) is contained in the Inheritance Tax Act
(IHTA) 1984.
• IHT is payable on transfers in excess of a nil rate band, currently £325,000 (2026/27).
• The residence nil rate band (RNRB) is in addition to the £325,000 nil rate band, and
is intended to protect, at least partially, the family home from IHT. It is £175,000 in
2026/27.
• Individuals who are long-term resident in the UK are liable to IHT on all their property,
whether it is in the UK or elsewhere.
Lifetime transfers and transfers on death
• A lifetime transfer can be exempt, a potentially exempt transfer (PET) or a chargeable
lifetime transfer (CLT).
• The annual exemption is £3,000. This means that a transferor may make lifetime
transfers exempt from IHT up to a total value of £3,000 in any one tax year.
• A PET is a lifetime transfer by an individual to another individual, a bare/absolute trust
or a disabled trust. 
• The consequences of making a PET are that:
– No tax is charged at the date of the gift.
– The gift does not have to be reported to HMRC.
– If the donor then survives for seven years, the gift becomes fully exempt and
escapes tax entirely.
• The most common chargeable transfers occur on lifetime gifts to trusts other than
trusts for a disabled person. A CLT results in a tax charge if it takes the donor’s
seven-year cumulation over the nil rate band. Tax is payable at 20% on the excess
over the nil rate band and there will be no further IHT to pay if the donor survives for
the next seven years.
• IHT is chargeable on the death of an individual. The tax is chargeable as if the
deceased had made a transfer of value equal to the value of their estate immediately
before death.
• A reduced IHT rate of 36% applies to an estate where at least 10% of the net estate is
left to charity.
• Any unused nil rate band on a person’s death can be transferred to the estate of their
surviving spouse or registered civil partner. This must be claimed on the second death
and is subject to an upper limit.
Reliefs
• Business relief is a relief for transfers of business property, which has to be owned for
two years to qualify for relief. Relief is available for lifetime transfers and on death.
• Agricultural relief is a relief for transfers of agricultural property. Relief is available for
lifetime transfers and on death.
• There is a combined limit of £2.5m, known as the 100% relief allowance, on the
amount of business property and agricultural property that can qualify for 100% IHT
relief. For assets in excess of £2.5m, relief is only at the rate of 50%.
• Woodlands relief is a special relief for growing timber.
Gifts with reservation
• A gift is treated as a gift with a reservation of benefit if it is not enjoyed to the exclusion
or virtual exclusion of the donor.
• If the donor still retains a benefit in the gift at death, its value is treated as remaining in
the donor’s estate and is taxed accordingly.

Key points
• The gift with reservation provisions do not apply to gifts that are exempt because they
are transfers between spouses or civil partners, small gifts, gifts in consideration of
marriage or civil partnership, gifts to charity, gifts to political parties and gifts for the
national benefit.
Pre-owned assets tax
• The spread of schemes to enable people to avoid the gift with reservation provisions
prompted the introduction of a pre-owned asset tax (POAT) charge, which came into
effect on 6 April 2005, but can apply to arrangements put in place earlier.
• Assets that may be caught by POAT are land, chattels and intangible assets comprised
in a settlement where the settlor retains an interest. In practice, this mainly covers life
assurance policies and has implications for some IHT planning arrangements.
• No tax is charged in any year in which the cash value of benefits is £5,000 or less.
• Individuals whose arrangements are caught by POAT might be able to undo them.
However, this may not be possible with some trust-based plans, especially where any
of the beneficiaries of a trust are minors. Another way of avoiding the tax is by electing
for the asset to be subject to IHT on death.
Valuation of the transfer
• The value of an asset for IHT purposes is generally taken as the price it might
reasonably be expected to fetch if it were sold on the open market.
• HMRC looks carefully at the valuations of transfers between connected parties, even
if they are claimed to be commercial transactions, to ensure that there is no element
of gift.
• CLTs have to be grossed up where the transferor pays the tax. 
Liability to inheritance tax
• The provisions for payment of IHT depend on whether the tax arises on a lifetime
transfer or on death.
• IHT on a CLT is primarily charged on the transferor, although it can be paid by the
transferee instead.
• If a transferor dies within seven years of a PET or chargeable gift, any extra tax due is
payable by the transferee six months after the end of the month in which death occurs.
• Tax payable on the estate of a deceased person is the liability of the legal personal
representatives (LPRs). Tax is due six months after the end of the month in which
death occurs.
Inheritance tax and relevant property trusts
• Tax charge on creation:
– Include any other relevant property trusts created by the same settlor in the last
seven years.
– Deduct any available nil rate band.
– Tax is 20% on excess over available nil rate band.
• Capital distributions – either in part or all – give rise to an IHT exit charge.
• Periodic charges arise on each ten-year anniversary.
• The maximum periodic charge is 6% on excess over available nil rate band.
