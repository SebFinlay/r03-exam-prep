1Income Tax

The shape of every income tax computation

Every income tax question follows the same skeleton. You gather up income by type, deduct any allowable reliefs, take off the personal allowance, and then tax what remains in strict layers: non-savings income first, then savings income, then dividends. The layering is not cosmetic. Each type of income has its own rates and its own allowances, and because income stacks in a fixed order, a pound of dividend income can be pushed into a higher band by salary sitting underneath it. Most of the marks in a computation question come from respecting this order.

Non-savings income means earnings, pensions, trading profits and rental income. Savings income is mainly interest, and also includes chargeable gains on life assurance bonds. Dividends sit on top. Bond gains behave as the top slice for the relief calculation you will meet in section 10.

The personal allowance and its trap

The personal allowance is £12,570, frozen at that level for some years yet, and this freeze quietly drags more people into tax each year as wages rise. Once your adjusted net income passes £100,000 the allowance is withdrawn at £1 for every £2 of excess, so it has vanished entirely by £125,140. Inside that window each extra £100 of income costs £40 of tax plus £20 from the lost allowance, an effective rate of 60%. Pension contributions and gift aid donations reduce adjusted net income, which is why they are the standard advice for anyone in this band.

The marriage allowance lets a spouse or civil partner who cannot use all of their personal allowance transfer £1,260 of it to their partner, worth up to £252 as a tax reduction. It is only available where neither party pays tax above the basic rate. Do not confuse it with the married couple's allowance, which exists only for couples where at least one partner was born before 6 April 1935.

Exam trapThe dividend rates rose by two percentage points from April 2026, so 2026/27 dividends are taxed at 10.75%, 35.75% and 39.35%. Savings and property income rates are due to rise by two points from April 2027, but for 2026/27 they remain at 20%, 40% and 45%. An exam question set in 2026/27 uses the old savings rates and the new dividend rates.

Both gift aid donations and personal pension contributions are paid net of 20% basic rate relief, and higher and additional rate relief is given by extending the basic rate (and higher rate) band by the gross amount. A £8,000 net pension contribution becomes £10,000 gross and pushes the £37,700 threshold up to £47,700, so £10,000 more income is taxed at basic instead of higher rates.

Savings income: two allowances that are not really allowances

Two 0% bands apply to interest. The starting rate for savings taxes the first £5,000 of savings income at 0%, but only to the extent that taxable non-savings income has not already filled that £5,000. Anyone with a salary above £17,570 loses it entirely, which is why it matters mainly for pensioners and part-time earners living off interest. On top of that, the personal savings allowance gives basic rate taxpayers £1,000 of interest at 0% and higher rate taxpayers £500; additional rate taxpayers get nothing. Both are technically nil-rate bands rather than deductions: the income still counts as taxable income, still fills the bands, and can still push other income upwards.

Dividends work the same way. The dividend allowance taxes the first £500 of dividends at 0% for everyone, whatever their marginal rate, but that £500 still occupies band space.

Worked example — why the 0% bands still use up band space

Priya has a salary of £48,000 and receives £600 of dividends in 2026/27.

If Priya's salary had been £50,000, the £500 allowance would have straddled the £37,700 threshold and the final £100 of dividends would have fallen into the higher band at 35.75%. The allowance taxed the dividends at 0% but did nothing to stop them climbing the bands.

Other pieces the exam likes

The high income child benefit charge claws back child benefit where the higher earner in a household has adjusted net income over £60,000, at 1% of the benefit for every £200 of excess, so the benefit has gone entirely by £80,000. The charge falls on the higher earner regardless of who receives the benefit. Separately, everyone has a £1,000 trading allowance and a £1,000 property allowance: gross income below those figures is simply not taxed and need not be reported, and above them you can choose to deduct the allowance instead of actual expenses.

Employment benefits in kind are taxed on their cash equivalent, with company cars valued as a CO2-based percentage of list price. Employees are taxed on the arising basis through PAYE, and the season-ticket loans threshold, trivial benefits and similar details are worth a skim of your card deck rather than deep study here.

Key pointWhen a computation goes wrong, it is nearly always an ordering error. Write the layers out every time: non-savings, then savings, then dividends, with bond gains as the top slice. Check which 0% bands apply, remember they consume band space, and only then apply rates.

2National Insurance Contributions

The four classes

National Insurance looks intimidating because of its class structure, but the logic is simple: employees and employers pay Class 1 on earnings, the self-employed pay Class 4 on profits, and Classes 2 and 3 exist only as voluntary top-ups for people protecting their State Pension record. NICs are charged on earnings, not on investment income, which is one reason a pound of salary and a pound of interest are treated so differently by the tax system as a whole.

Class 1 (secondary)Employers15% on all earnings above £96 a week — no upper limit

Class 2Self-employed (voluntary)Flat £3.65 a week, paid voluntarily to protect benefit entitlement

The weekly employee thresholds annualise to the same figures you know from income tax: the £242 primary threshold is £12,570 a year and the £967 upper earnings limit is £50,270. The employer's secondary threshold is far lower, £96 a week (about £5,000 a year), and unlike the employee there is no upper limit: the employer keeps paying 15% all the way up. Most employers can offset an employment allowance of £10,500 a year against their secondary Class 1 bill, though a company whose only employee is its sole director cannot claim it.

Edges and wrinkles

Employees stop paying NICs at State Pension age, though their employer carries on paying secondary contributions. For the self-employed, Class 4 stops from the start of the tax year in which SPA is reached if it was reached before the year began. Special upper secondary thresholds mean employers pay nothing for employees under 21, apprentices under 25 and armed forces veterans in their first civilian year, on earnings up to £967 a week. Company directors have an annual earnings period, so a director cannot avoid NICs by taking salary as a single lump: the annual thresholds of £12,570 and £50,270 apply to the year's earnings as a whole.

Class 4 contributions buy no benefits whatsoever, which is why paying them unnecessarily should be avoided. Class 2, now purely voluntary at £3.65 a week, is the cheap route by which a low-profit self-employed person protects their State Pension entitlement; Class 3 at £18.40 a week does the same job for anyone else and can be paid up to six years after the year in question.

Exam trapNICs are charged per employment. Someone with two part-time jobs, each below £242 a week, pays nothing even if the combined earnings would attract contributions in a single job. Investment income never attracts NICs at any level.

3Capital Gains Tax

What is and is not caught

CGT taxes the profit on disposing of an asset: a sale, but equally a gift, since giving something away is a disposal at market value. Death is not a disposal. Assets pass to the estate at their date-of-death value with the gain wiped out, the so-called uplift on death, and this single fact drives a great deal of later-life planning: an elderly investor holding a large gain may be better advised to keep the asset until death than to sell or gift it and crystallise CGT.

A surprising amount is exempt. Your main residence, cars, gilts, qualifying corporate bonds, ISA holdings, EIS and SEIS shares held for three years, VCT shares, betting winnings, and chattels sold for £6,000 or less all escape entirely. Wasting chattels (tangible movable property with a life of 50 years or less) are exempt whatever their value. For chattels sold above £6,000 there is a marginal rule: the gain cannot exceed 5/3 of the excess of proceeds over £6,000. Transfers between spouses and civil partners are made on a no gain, no loss basis, meaning the recipient inherits the original base cost — not an exemption so much as a deferral, and the standard first move in couples' planning.

The computation

The mechanics are short: proceeds (or market value for gifts), less acquisition cost, less incidental costs of purchase and sale and any enhancement expenditure. Current-year losses must be set against current-year gains in full, even if that wastes the annual exempt amount. Brought-forward losses are kinder: you use only enough of them to bring net gains down to the annual exempt amount, and carry the rest onward.

CGT figure (2026/27)Amount

Gains stack on top of taxable income to determine the rate. You may set the annual exempt amount and losses against gains in whatever order saves most tax; with both 18% and 24% gains in a year, set them against the 24% gains first. The basic rate band can be extended by gross pension contributions and gift aid, which lowers the CGT rate on gains that would otherwise fall above £37,700, a point examiners enjoy.

Worked example — losses, the exempt amount and the rate bands

Matching rules for shares

Shares of the same class in the same company are pooled at average cost, but two anti-avoidance rules take priority. A disposal is matched first with any shares bought the same day, then with shares bought in the following 30 days, and only then with the section 104 pool. The 30-day rule exists to kill "bed and breakfasting": selling shares purely to crystallise a gain or loss and buying them back the next morning. Selling and repurchasing inside an ISA, or having your spouse buy the equivalent holding, still achieves the same effect legitimately.

Reliefs

Private residence relief exempts the gain on your only or main home for the periods you lived in it, plus the final nine months of ownership regardless. Certain absences count as deemed occupation if the property was your main residence before and after. A couple can only have one main residence between them, and where you own two homes you can elect which qualifies within two years of the combination changing.

Business asset disposal relief taxes the first £1m of lifetime qualifying gains at a flat 18%: broadly disposals of all or part of a trading business owned for two years, or shares in your personal trading company (5% voting and economic interest, and you are an officer or employee). Investors' relief extends similar treatment to long-term external investors with a separate £1m limit. Holdover relief lets a gift of business assets, or any gift that is immediately chargeable to IHT such as a transfer into a discretionary trust, be made without CGT: the donee takes over the donor's base cost and the gain waits. EIS investment lets you defer any gain by reinvesting it; SEIS reinvestment exempts half of the reinvested gain outright.

Exam trapSales of UK residential property need a return and a payment on account of the CGT within 60 days of completion — not the following 31 January. And remember the annual exempt amount is lost if unused; it never carries forward, which is the argument for realising gains up to £3,000 every year.

4Inheritance Tax

How IHT thinks about a gift

Inheritance tax is not really a tax on inheriting. It is a tax on transfers of value: any occasion on which your estate becomes worth less because you have given something away. Death is simply treated as one final transfer of everything you own. This framing matters because the tax measures the loss to your estate, not the value of what the recipient gains. If you own a pair of antique vases worth £50,000 together but only £15,000 each on their own, giving one away costs your estate £35,000, and that is the figure IHT works with, even though the recipient only received something worth £15,000.

There are only three rates to remember: 0% within the nil rate band, 20% on chargeable lifetime transfers, and 40% on death. The 20% lifetime rate is deliberately half the death rate. Almost everything else in this section is about working out which rate applies, and to how much.

The nil rate bands

Everyone has a nil rate band (NRB) of £325,000, frozen at that level until 5 April 2031. Transfers within it are taxed at 0%, but they still use it up, and that is the mechanism behind most IHT calculations: gifts made in the seven years before a transfer stack up ("cumulate") and eat into the band available for whatever comes next.

Band / rate (2026/27)Amount
Nil rate band (NRB)£325,000 — frozen to 5 April 2031
Residence nil rate band (RNRB)£175,000 — frozen to 5 April 2031
Death rate40% (36% where ≥10% of the net estate goes to charity)
Lifetime rate (CLTs)20% (25% of the net gift if the donor pays the tax)
RNRB taper threshold£2m — RNRB reduced £1 for every £2 over

When the first of a married couple (or civil partners) dies, any proportion of the NRB they did not use transfers to the survivor. It is the unused percentage that carries over, applied to the band in force at the second death, and the uplift is capped at 100%. A widow whose husband used none of his band therefore has £650,000 of nil rate band available on her own death.

The residence nil rate band is an extra £175,000 that applies only where a home the deceased lived in passes on death to direct descendants: children (including step, adopted and foster children), grandchildren, and their spouses or civil partners. It too is transferable between spouses, even if the first death was before the RNRB existed in April 2017, and even if the first estate had no home in it. Two features catch people out. First, it tapers away for estates over £2m: you lose £1 of RNRB for every £2 of net estate above the threshold, so an estate of £2.1m has its RNRB cut to £125,000 and by £2.35m a single person's RNRB has gone entirely. Second, the RNRB never applies to lifetime gifts that become chargeable on death; it shelters the death estate only.

Exam trapThe £2m RNRB taper is measured against the estate before reliefs and exemptions are deducted. An estate can be far below £2m after business relief and still lose its RNRB. And a buy-to-let the deceased never lived in can never qualify, however clearly it passes to the children.

Exemptions

Transfers between spouses and civil partners are completely exempt, in life and on death, which is why IHT planning for couples is really about the second death. (Where the recipient spouse is not a long-term UK resident for IHT purposes, the exemption is capped at an amount equal to the NRB.) Gifts to UK charities and qualifying political parties are also wholly exempt. The remaining exemptions apply to lifetime gifts only, and they are worth memorising as a set:

Lifetime exemptionAmount / condition
Annual exemption£3,000 per tax year; one year's unused balance can be carried forward, and the current year's is used first
Small gifts£250 per recipient per tax year — cannot be combined with any other exemption for the same recipient
Wedding gifts£5,000 from a parent; £2,500 from a grandparent (or from one party to the other); £1,000 from anyone else
Normal expenditure out of incomeUnlimited, if part of a regular pattern, made from income, and leaving the donor's usual standard of living intact

Normal expenditure out of income is the most powerful of these and the least understood. There is no monetary ceiling. What HMRC looks for is a settled pattern (regular premiums on a policy in trust are the classic case), funding from income rather than capital, and no drop in the donor's own standard of living. The amounts do not have to be identical each year.

Exam trapThe £250 small gifts exemption is all or nothing. A gift of £3,250 to one person cannot be split into £3,000 annual exemption plus £250 small gift; once a gift to that person exceeds £250, the small gifts exemption is lost for them entirely.

PETs and CLTs

Any lifetime gift that is not exempt falls into one of two categories, and classifying it correctly is the first step in every lifetime-transfer question. A potentially exempt transfer (PET) is a gift to another individual, to a bare trust, or to a disabled trust. Nothing is payable when you make it; if you survive seven years it becomes fully exempt, and if you die within seven years it becomes chargeable retrospectively. A chargeable lifetime transfer (CLT) is essentially any gift into a trust other than those two kinds, most commonly a discretionary trust. A CLT is taxed immediately at 20% on whatever exceeds your available nil rate band, with more tax potentially due if you die within seven years.

One refinement on the CLT charge: the 20% rate assumes the trustees pay the tax. If you pay it yourself, your estate has lost both the gift and the tax, so the gift is "grossed up" and the effective rate becomes 25% of the net amount given.

Death within seven years: taper relief

When you die within seven years of a PET or CLT, the transfer is taxed (or re-taxed) at death rates, using the nil rate band and rates in force at death, but the value of the gift and your seven-year cumulation as they stood at the date of the gift. If you survived at least three years, taper relief then reduces the tax bill. Read the timeline as the percentage of the full 40% charge that remains payable:

100%0–3 yrs
80%3–4

40%5–6
20%6–7
0%7+
Proportion of the full death-rate tax still payable, by years survived after the gift

Three points about taper relief repay careful attention. It reduces the tax, never the value of the transfer, so a tapered gift still occupies the nil rate band in full when you calculate tax on later transfers and on the estate. It is worthless where the gift sits within the nil rate band, because there is no tax to taper; the gift's only effect is to soak up nil rate band that the estate would otherwise have used. And for a CLT on which 20% was already paid in lifetime, the lifetime tax is credited against the death charge; in years six and seven the tapered charge (8% effective) is below the 20% already paid, so no more is due, but nothing is refunded either.

Margaret made two lifetime gifts, after all exemptions: a CLT of £150,000 into a discretionary trust in May 2019, and a PET of £200,000 to her daughter in June 2022. She dies in January 2027 leaving an estate of £600,000, including her home worth £250,000, all to her children. Assume no transferable bands from a spouse.

Gifts with reservation

You cannot give something away and keep enjoying it. If you gift your house to your children but carry on living in it rent-free, the gift is a gift with reservation of benefit: for IHT the house stays in your estate at its value on your death, however long ago you gave it away. The reservation can end, and the seven-year clock then starts from that point; equally a gift can be clean at the outset and become reserved later, for instance if you stop paying a full market rent. Paying a genuine market rent, or giving away an undivided share while sharing the running costs of a home you occupy together, keeps the gift clean. The rules bite only for IHT; the gift remains perfectly valid as a matter of property law. Where the reservation rules do not catch an arrangement, the standalone pre-owned assets tax (an income tax charge on the benefit retained) may apply instead.

Business relief, agricultural relief and quick succession relief

Business relief exists so that a family business does not have to be broken up to pay the tax bill on it. Relief at 100% covers an unincorporated business or a share in one, and unquoted shareholdings of any size, including AIM shares until 5 April 2026. Relief at 50% covers controlling holdings in fully listed companies, land or plant used by your company or partnership, and, from 6 April 2026, AIM shares. The asset must generally have been owned for two years, and businesses that mainly deal in investments, land or shares do not qualify at all. From 6 April 2026 a significant new limit applies: 100% relief is capped by a £1m allowance per person (shared across business and agricultural property); qualifying value above the allowance gets 50% relief instead. Agricultural relief works on similar lines for farmland and farm buildings, on the agricultural value only.

Quick succession relief deals with the unfairness of the same wealth being taxed twice in short order. If you die within five years of inheriting property that bore IHT, the tax on your own estate is reduced by a sliding percentage of the tax attributable to the earlier transfer:

Gap between the two chargesQSR percentage
Up to 1 year100%
1–2 years80%
2–3 years60%
3–4 years40%
4–5 years20%

The death estate, the charity rate, and paying the tax

The estate on death is everything owned at market value, less debts and reasonable funeral expenses. A share of a home held as joint tenants passes automatically to the co-owner but the deceased's share still counts in the estate. Where at least 10% of the "baseline" net estate (broadly, the estate after liabilities, reliefs, exemptions and the ordinary NRB, but before the RNRB and before the charitable legacy itself) is left to charity, the whole taxable estate is charged at 36% instead of 40%.

Tax on the estate is due six months after the end of the month of death, and the personal representatives pay it; interest runs after that. Tax on a lifetime CLT is due six months after the end of the month of transfer, except that transfers made between 6 April and 30 September fall due on the following 30 April. Extra tax arising because the donor died within seven years of a gift is payable by the donee. Tax on land, controlling shareholdings and business property can be paid in ten equal annual instalments, though the option lapses if the asset is sold.

Key pointIn the exam, always fix the order of a death computation: classify each lifetime gift, deduct exemptions, tax gifts oldest first using each gift's own seven-year look-back for cumulation, apply taper to any tax due, then tax the estate using whatever NRB the last seven years of gifts have left behind, plus RNRB if a home passes to direct descendants.

5Residence & Domicile

Why residence matters

UK residents are taxed on their worldwide income and gains as they arise. Non-residents are broadly taxed only on UK-source income, and generally escape CGT altogether except on UK land and property. Residence is decided year by year under the statutory residence test (SRT), which works through three stages in a fixed order. If any stage produces an answer, you stop.

The statutory residence test — three stages, applied in order

The ties are family (spouse, partner or minor children resident here), accommodation available to you, substantive work in the UK, more than 90 days spent here in either of the two previous tax years, and, for leavers only, spending more days in the UK than in any other single country. The more days you spend here, the fewer ties you can afford. Arrivers (not resident in any of the previous three years) ignore the country tie; leavers count all five, which makes it deliberately harder to shed residence than to acquire it. Days are counted by presence at midnight, with a deeming rule to stop day-trip manipulation.

A tax year is normally all-or-nothing, but split year treatment can divide the year into a resident part and a non-resident part when you arrive or leave partway through in defined circumstances, such as starting full-time work abroad. And someone who leaves for a short spell cannot simply realise gains abroad: if you return within five years, gains realised while away are taxed in the year of return under the temporary non-residence rules.

The FIG regime: what replaced domicile

Domicile no longer drives UK tax. Since April 2025 the old remittance basis has been replaced by the four-year foreign income and gains (FIG) regime. A qualifying new resident, someone arriving after at least ten consecutive tax years of non-residence, can claim for any or all of their first four years of residence to have their foreign income and gains completely exempt, even if the money is brought to the UK. The price of a claim is real: for that year they lose their personal allowance and CGT annual exempt amount. UK income and gains are taxed normally regardless. Eligible employees can also claim overseas workday relief on the same four-year footing.

Exam trapThe FIG clock runs on tax years of residence, not on claims. Someone who claims in years one and three but not two still has only four years in total from arrival, and years they skip are not added on at the end.

IHT: long-term residence

IHT has also switched from domicile to a residence test. You are a long-term UK resident, and therefore within IHT on your worldwide assets, once you have been UK resident for at least 10 of the previous 20 tax years. Anyone else is within IHT only on UK-situs assets. Shedding the status takes time on the way out too: a leaver remains long-term resident for a tail of between three and ten years depending on how long they were resident. The spouse exemption is unlimited between two long-term residents, but capped at the level of the nil rate band where the recipient spouse is not one, though the recipient can elect to be treated as long-term resident to unlock the full exemption.

Key pointKeep the two residence tests apart in your mind. The SRT decides each single year for income tax and CGT. Long-term residence is a rolling 10-out-of-20-year count that decides worldwide IHT exposure. A person can easily be non-resident under the SRT this year yet still be a long-term resident for IHT.

6Self Assessment & Anti-Avoidance

The self assessment cycle

Self assessment shifts the burden of getting tax right onto the taxpayer. For any tax year, a paper return is due by 31 October following the year end and an online return by the following 31 January. File on paper by 31 October and HMRC will calculate the tax for you; underpayments of up to £3,000 can be collected through a later PAYE code if the return is in by 30 December online.

Income tax and Class 4 NICs are normally paid in three instalments. Two payments on account, each half of the previous year's liability, fall due on 31 January in the tax year and the following 31 July, with a balancing payment (or repayment) the next 31 January, which is also when any CGT is due. Nobody makes payments on account if last year's bill (net of tax deducted at source) was under £1,000, or if more than 80% of their tax was collected at source. You can claim to reduce payments on account if you expect a lower liability, but reduce them too far and interest, and possibly a penalty, follows.

Penalties and interest

Late filing costs £100 automatically, even if no tax is due. Once a return is three months late, daily £10 penalties run for up to 90 days; at six months, and again at twelve, a further penalty of the higher of £300 and 5% of the tax due is added, so a year's delay costs at least £1,600 before any tax-geared penalties. Late payment is punished separately: a 5% surcharge on tax still unpaid 30 days after the due date, repeated at six and twelve months, with interest running throughout on top. Errors in returns attract penalties scaled to behaviour, from nothing for a careful mistake disclosed promptly up to 100% of the tax for deliberate and concealed understatement, and HMRC's window to raise a discovery assessment stretches from four years for innocent error through six for carelessness to twenty for deliberate conduct.

Exam trapPayments on account never include CGT. However large a gain, the CGT is due in one amount on 31 January after the tax year (subject to the 60-day payment on account for UK residential property), and it does not feed into the following year's payments on account either.

Avoidance, evasion and the rules between

Evasion is illegal concealment: not declaring income, falsifying figures. Avoidance uses legal means to reduce tax, but Parliament has built machinery to catch the aggressive end of it. The DOTAS regime forces promoters to disclose notifiable avoidance schemes to HMRC early, and users must quote the scheme reference number on their returns. Accelerated payment notices can require disputed tax to be paid up front while a scheme is litigated. Above all this sits the general anti-abuse rule (GAAR), which lets HMRC counteract tax advantages from arrangements that are "abusive", meaning they cannot reasonably be regarded as a reasonable course of action in relation to the legislation (the "double reasonableness" test). It applies to income tax, CGT, IHT, corporation tax, NICs and SDLT, but not VAT, and carries a 60% penalty of the counteracted advantage.

Key pointUsing ISAs, pensions and the annual exemptions is neither avoidance nor evasion; it is using the system as intended. The exam wants you to place any scenario on the spectrum: legitimate planning, disclosable avoidance, abusive arrangement caught by the GAAR, or criminal evasion.

Taxes on share purchases

Buying shares electronically through CREST attracts stamp duty reserve tax at 0.5% of the consideration, rounded to the nearest penny and collected automatically. Paper stock transfer forms attract stamp duty at 0.5% rounded up to the nearest £5, with no charge where the consideration is £1,000 or less. Both fall on the purchaser. Gilts, most corporate bonds, new issues, AIM shares and units in collective investment schemes are outside the charge, and gifts with no consideration attract nothing.

Exam trapThe 0.5% is charged on the price paid, not the market value, and it is the buyer who pays; there is no stamp duty on the seller's side. ETFs and AIM shares are the classic "no stamp duty" answers in comparison questions.

8VAT & Corporation Tax

VAT essentials

VAT is a tax on consumer spending collected in stages by businesses. A registered trader charges output tax on sales, reclaims input tax on purchases, and pays HMRC the difference, so the burden lands on the final consumer. Registration is compulsory once taxable supplies in the previous twelve months exceed £90,000, or as soon as you expect to exceed £90,000 in the next 30 days alone; deregistration is possible below £88,000. Voluntary registration below the threshold can make sense for a business selling to VAT-registered customers, because it unlocks input tax recovery at no real cost to those customers.

The distinction the exam tests hardest is zero-rated versus exempt. Both mean no VAT on the sale, but a zero-rated trader (food, books, children's clothing, new homes) is fully within the system and reclaims all input tax, while an exempt trader (finance, insurance, education, health) cannot register in respect of those supplies and the input VAT sticks as a cost. Most financial advice and intermediation is exempt, which is why VAT recovery is a live issue for advisory firms. The standard rate is 20%, with 5% on domestic fuel and a few other reduced-rate items.

Corporation tax

Companies pay corporation tax on their profits, meaning trading income, investment income and chargeable gains together. The main rate is 25%, but a small profits rate of 19% applies where profits do not exceed £50,000, with marginal relief between £50,000 and £250,000. The effect of the relief is that profits in the marginal zone bear 19% on the first £50,000 and 26.5% on the slice between £50,000 and £250,000, so the marginal pound in that zone is taxed more heavily than in either band around it. These limits are divided among associated companies. Dividends a company receives from other companies are generally exempt, and dividends it pays are not deductible: they come out of taxed profits.

For the adviser, corporation tax matters mainly because it shapes the internal tax drag on funds: life company policyholder funds bear corporation tax at broadly 20% on income and gains, which is the root of the onshore bond's "tax paid" treatment you will meet in section 10.

Exam trapZero-rated and exempt are not synonyms. If a question asks which trader can reclaim input VAT despite charging no VAT, the answer is the zero-rated one, never the exempt one.

9Direct Investments

Cash and interest-bearing assets

Bank and building society interest is paid gross and taxed as savings income, sheltered first by the starting rate for savings and the personal savings allowance from section 1. NS&I products split into the taxable (income bonds, guaranteed bonds) and the wholly tax-free (premium bond prizes, fixed-interest and index-linked savings certificates), and the tax-free ones are worth proportionately more to a higher rate taxpayer, a comparison the exam runs regularly.

Gilts have a distinctive split personality. The interest is taxable as savings income, paid gross by default, but any capital gain is completely exempt from CGT, and losses are correspondingly not allowable. Corporate bonds that meet the conditions to be qualifying corporate bonds enjoy the same CGT exemption. This makes low-coupon gilts standing below par attractive to higher rate taxpayers, who convert taxable interest into exempt capital growth. The accrued income scheme stops the obvious abuse of selling a bond just before the coupon date to dress interest up as capital: on a sale, the accrued interest is taxed as income of the seller, though only where nominal holdings exceed £5,000.

Equities

Dividends are taxed as the top layer of income apart from bond gains, at 10.75%, 35.75% or 39.35% after the £500 dividend allowance. Disposals are within CGT with the share matching rules from section 3. Stamp duty or SDRT at 0.5% applies on purchase. There is nothing exotic here; equities are the baseline against which the wrappers in section 10 are compared.

Buy-to-let property

Rental profits are non-savings income, normally computed on the cash basis, with deductions for the running costs of the letting: agents' fees, repairs (but not improvements), insurance and so on. The rule that catches candidates is mortgage interest. Interest on a loan to buy a residential let is not deductible from rents at all; instead you get a basic rate tax reducer of 20% of the interest. A higher rate taxpayer with substantial interest costs can therefore show a taxable profit far larger than their cash profit, and the reducer cannot create a refund. Two small shelters exist: the £1,000 property allowance for micro landlords, and rent-a-room relief exempting up to £7,500 of gross receipts from letting a furnished room in your own home (halved where two people share the income). On sale, gains are within CGT with the 60-day reporting deadline, and no business asset disposal relief, because ordinary letting is an investment, not a trade.

Exam trapThe 20% mortgage interest reducer applies to residential lets only; interest on commercial property loans remains fully deductible. And repairs restore, improvements enhance: redecorating is deductible against rent, an extension is capital and only helps in the CGT computation on sale.

10Indirect Investments

ISAs

The ISA is the simplest wrapper: no tax on income or gains inside, nothing to report, no tax on withdrawal. The overall subscription limit is £20,000 a year across the adult ISAs, with a Junior ISA limit of £9,000 and a Lifetime ISA limit of £4,000 (which counts within the £20,000). The Lifetime ISA, open to those aged 18 to 39, adds a 25% government bonus but charges a 25% penalty on withdrawals that are not for a first home costing up to £450,000 or for age 60 onwards, and that penalty claws back slightly more than the bonus. Losses inside an ISA are never allowable, ISAs cannot be held jointly, and on death the funds keep their tax-free status while the estate is administered; a surviving spouse also receives an additional permitted subscription equal to the deceased's ISA value, on top of their own allowance. From April 2027 the cash ISA limit falls to £12,000 for savers under 65, with the overall £20,000 unchanged.

Collectives and REITs

Unit trusts and OEICs are taxed transparently at the investor level: equity funds pay dividend distributions taxed at dividend rates, funds more than 60% in interest-bearing assets pay interest distributions taxed as savings income, and reinvested (accumulation) income is still taxable even though nothing is received. The fund itself pays no tax on gains it makes internally, and the investor faces CGT only on disposing of their own units. Investment trusts behave like ordinary shares. Real estate investment trusts are tax-transparent for their property business: the property income distribution (PID) is paid net of 20% and taxed on you as property income, not as a dividend, while ordinary dividends from the residual business are taxed normally; gains on the shares are within CGT as usual.

Life assurance bonds and the 5% rule

A single-premium investment bond is a non-qualifying life policy. Nothing you receive from it is ever within CGT; instead, chargeable event gains are taxed as savings income when a chargeable event happens: death, maturity, full surrender, assignment for money, or a part surrender beyond the allowance. The 5% rule is what makes bonds a planning tool: each policy year you can withdraw up to 5% of the original premium with no immediate tax at all, unused amounts carrying forward, until 100% of the premium has been returned. The 5% withdrawals are tax-deferred, not tax-free; they are added back when the bond finally ends.

Onshore and offshore versions differ in one fundamental respect. An onshore fund has already suffered life company tax of broadly 20%, so the investor receives a 20% credit: basic rate taxpayers owe nothing more, and higher and additional rate payers top up to 40% or 45%. An offshore fund rolls up gross, so the whole gain is taxable at up to 45% with no credit, and the price of gross roll-up is a bigger bill at the end.

Top-slicing relief

A bond gain built up over many years lands in a single tax year and can push a basic rate taxpayer deep into higher rates. Top-slicing relief limits the damage by asking what tax you would have paid had the gain arrived evenly. The method is five fixed steps: work out the tax on the full gain in the year; deduct the 20% treated as paid to find the liability; divide the gain by N, the number of complete policy years (or years since the last chargeable event), to get the annual equivalent; tax that slice, deduct 20% of it, and multiply back up by N to get the relieved liability; the relief is the difference between the two liabilities.

Worked example — top-slicing on an onshore bond

Dev invested £50,000 in an onshore bond and fully surrenders it after six complete policy years for £74,000, a gain of £24,000. His other income in 2026/27 is £48,570, all non-savings, so his taxable income before the gain is £36,000 and he has £1,700 of basic rate band left. As a higher rate taxpayer his personal savings allowance is £500.

Pensions

Pensions remain the most heavily incentivised wrapper: contributions attract relief at your marginal rate, the fund grows almost tax free, and a quarter can be drawn tax free at retirement. Tax-relievable personal contributions are limited to the higher of £3,600 and 100% of relevant UK earnings; on top sits the annual allowance of £60,000 covering employer and employee contributions together, with unused allowance from the three previous years available under carry forward. High earners see the allowance tapered by £1 for every £2 of adjusted income over £260,000, down to a floor of £10,000 by £360,000. Flexibly accessing a money purchase pot triggers the money purchase annual allowance of £10,000 and kills carry forward for money purchase savings. Exceed whichever allowance applies and an annual allowance charge at your marginal rate claws the relief back.

The tax-free lump sum is normally 25% of the fund, capped by the lump sum allowance of £268,275. Income drawn beyond it, whether by annuity, drawdown or the taxable 75% of an UFPLS payment, is non-savings income through PAYE. Death benefits are currently outside the estate for IHT, but from 6 April 2027 unused pension funds will be brought within it, a change that reverses the recent practice of preserving pensions and spending other assets first.

Exam trapPersonal contributions are limited by earnings, and dividends, rent and interest are not earnings. A company director paid mainly in dividends may have a large income yet almost no scope for tax-relieved personal contributions; an employer contribution sidesteps the earnings test entirely, limited only by the annual allowance.

11Trust Taxation

Three trusts, three tax personalities

For tax purposes almost every trust you meet in R03 is one of three kinds. A bare (absolute) trust is barely a trust at all: the trustees are nominees, the named beneficiary is absolutely entitled, and all income and gains are simply taxed on the beneficiary with their own allowances. An interest in possession (IIP) trust gives a life tenant the right to income as it arises, with the capital preserved for others. A discretionary trust gives the trustees genuine choice over who benefits and when, and the tax system charges hardest for that flexibility.

Trustees never get a personal allowance, personal savings allowance or dividend allowance. Since April 2024 a trust whose income is £500 or less for the year simply pays nothing, replacing the old standard rate band. In an IIP trust the trustees' basic rate tax is effectively paid on the life tenant's behalf: a non-taxpaying beneficiary reclaims it, a higher rate beneficiary tops it up, and mandating income directly to the beneficiary avoids the trustee charge altogether. In a discretionary trust every income distribution reaches the beneficiary with a 45% credit regardless of the underlying income type, so a basic rate beneficiary can reclaim a substantial refund; the price is that the trustees must have actually paid enough 45% tax into their "tax pool" to cover the credits, and dividend income taxed at 39.35% leaves a shortfall the trustees must fund.

Exam trapThe parental settlement rule: where a parent settles funds on their own minor unmarried child, and the income exceeds £100 a year per parent per child, all of it is taxed as the parent's. This applies to bare trusts and outright gifts alike, and it is why grandparents are the tax-efficient settlors for grandchildren's savings.

Capital gains tax

Trustees pay CGT at 24% with an annual exempt amount of £1,500, half the individual figure, shared between trusts created by the same settlor (subject to a minimum). A transfer into a relevant property trust is a disposal by the settlor at market value, but because it is immediately chargeable to IHT, holdover relief is available whatever the asset, so the gain can travel into the trust with the trustees taking the settlor's base cost. The same relief works on the way out when assets are appointed to beneficiaries. Bare trusts have no trustee CGT at all: gains are the beneficiary's, with the beneficiary's own £3,000 exemption.

Inheritance tax: the relevant property regime

Section 4 established that a gift into a bare trust is a PET while gifts into IIP and discretionary trusts are CLTs. Once inside a relevant property trust, the fund faces its own IHT cycle: a periodic charge on each ten-year anniversary of up to 6% of the value above the available nil rate band, and an exit charge, a time-apportioned fraction of the periodic rate, when capital leaves between anniversaries. The trust's nil rate band is reduced by the settlor's chargeable transfers in the seven years before creation, so a settlor who had already used their band leaves the trust exposed from the start. Keeping the trust fund at or below the nil rate band at each ten-year point is the standard way to run these charges at nil, and even where that fails, a maximum of 6% a decade is often a price worth paying for control. The fund sits in no one's estate, which is the point.

Key pointPre-2006 interest in possession trusts, trusts created on death for a surviving spouse, and certain trusts for bereaved minors and disabled people sit outside the relevant property regime, with the trust assets treated as part of the relevant beneficiary's estate instead. For anything created in lifetime today, assume PET treatment for bare and disabled trusts and the relevant property regime for everything else.

Planning with income tax

Most personal tax planning is the unglamorous business of wasting nothing. Between spouses and civil partners, assets can move freely with no CGT or IHT cost, so income-producing investments belong with the lower-taxed partner, and each of the couple should be using their own personal allowance, savings allowance, dividend allowance, ISA subscription and CGT exemption before either doubles up. Pension contributions and gift aid extend the basic rate band, restore a tapered personal allowance, and can defuse the high income child benefit charge, giving effective relief far above the headline rates in the £60,000–£80,000 and £100,000–£125,140 corridors.

Timing matters this year more than most. Dividend rates rose by two percentage points in April 2026, and savings and property income rates follow in April 2027, so accelerating interest and rental income into 2026/27, for instance by choosing a maturity date or invoicing pattern deliberately, locks in the lower rates. Deferring income into a year when you expect to be in a lower band remains the mirror-image technique.

Planning with CGT and IHT

For CGT, use the £3,000 exemption every year or lose it, realise offsetting losses in the same tax year with care, spread large disposals across two tax years, and remember the interspousal transfer before a sale so both exemptions and both basic rate bands are in play. Deferring a disposal until death remains the bluntest but most effective relief of all, because of the CGT uplift. For IHT, the ordering is: spend and enjoy first, then give early and survive seven years, using the annual and normal-expenditure exemptions that cost nothing, and consider nil-rate-band discretionary trusts topped up at seven-year intervals for control. Keep an eye on the RNRB taper for estates near £2m, where a lifetime gift that brings the estate below the threshold can save 40% and recover tapered RNRB at the same time.
